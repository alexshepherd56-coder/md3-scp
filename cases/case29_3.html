Case 29.3 – Uterine Prolapse (Pelvic Organ Prolapse)

Category: Medicine | Discipline: Urogynaecology | Setting: General Practice

Clinical Scenario

Mrs. Patricia Reynolds, a 63-year-old postmenopausal woman, presents to her GP with a 12-month history of a sensation of "something coming down" in her vagina.

"Doctor, for the past year I've felt like something is bulging out of my vagina. It's worse by the end of the day, especially if I've been standing a lot. I can actually see and feel a lump coming out when I'm in the shower. It's really uncomfortable - I get a dragging sensation in my pelvis. It's also affecting my bowel movements - I sometimes have to push on the back wall of my vagina to help me empty my bowels."

History

  • Presenting complaint: Sensation of vaginal bulge ("something coming down") for 12 months, progressively worsening
  • Aggravating factors: Worse with prolonged standing, end of day, straining, coughing
  • Relieving factors: Improves when lying down
  • Associated symptoms:
    • Pelvic pressure/dragging sensation (heaviness in pelvis)
    • Difficulty with bowel emptying - requires digital vaginal splinting to defecate
    • Urinary symptoms: Difficulty initiating stream, feeling of incomplete emptying, no stress incontinence
    • Backache (worse end of day)
  • Obstetric history: Para 4 (4 vaginal deliveries, largest baby 4.5kg, two forceps deliveries, no tears)
  • Gynaecological history: Menopause age 51 (12 years ago), no HRT, no abnormal bleeding
  • Past medical history: Chronic constipation (lifelong), hypertension, osteoarthritis (knees)
  • Medications: Amlodipine 5mg OD, paracetamol PRN, lactulose (for constipation)
  • Social history: Non-smoker, BMI 31, retired cleaner (physically demanding job for 30 years)

Examination

  • Abdominal examination: Soft, non-tender, no masses, no organomegaly
  • Pelvic examination (lithotomy position):
    • At rest: Cervix visible at introitus (just inside vaginal opening)
    • With Valsalva (straining):
      • Uterine prolapse: Cervix descends 2cm beyond the hymen (procidentia)
      • Anterior vaginal wall prolapse (cystocele): Moderate anterior wall descent
      • Posterior vaginal wall prolapse (rectocele): Moderate posterior wall descent
    • Bimanual: Normal-sized postmenopausal uterus, mobile, no adnexal masses, no cervical excitation
    • Cervix: Healthy appearance, no ulceration or bleeding
  • Neurological examination (lower limbs): Normal

Investigations

  • Urine dipstick: Negative
  • Cervical screening: Up-to-date, last smear 2 years ago (normal, hrHPV negative)
  • Post-void residual volume: 110mL (elevated - normal <50mL) - suggests incomplete bladder emptying due to prolapse

Questions

1. What is the diagnosis? Describe the classification of pelvic organ prolapse and identify the risk factors present in this patient.

Diagnosis: Pelvic Organ Prolapse (POP) - Multicompartment

  • Uterine prolapse (uterovaginal prolapse) - Stage 3 (cervix descends >2cm beyond hymen)
  • Anterior vaginal wall prolapse (cystocele) - bladder descent
  • Posterior vaginal wall prolapse (rectocele) - rectal descent

Definition:

Pelvic organ prolapse (POP) = descent of one or more pelvic organs (uterus, bladder, rectum) from their normal anatomical position, causing a bulge into the vagina.

Classification of Pelvic Organ Prolapse:

A. By Anatomical Compartment:

Type Compartment Description Symptoms
Anterior prolapse (Cystocele) Anterior vaginal wall Bladder descends into anterior vaginal wall Urinary symptoms (difficulty initiating, incomplete emptying, stress incontinence)
Uterine prolapse (Uterovaginal) Apical (uterus/cervix) Uterus/cervix descends into vagina or beyond Sensation of bulge, pelvic pressure, backache
Vault prolapse Apical (post-hysterectomy) Vaginal vault descends (after hysterectomy) Sensation of bulge, pelvic pressure
Posterior prolapse (Rectocele) Posterior vaginal wall Rectum bulges into posterior vaginal wall Difficulty with bowel emptying, need for vaginal splinting (digitation)
Enterocele Apical/posterior (upper) Small bowel herniates into pouch of Douglas → vaginal vault Pelvic pressure (worse standing), usually asymptomatic

B. POP-Q Staging System (Pelvic Organ Prolapse Quantification):

Gold standard for clinical staging - measures descent of pelvic organs relative to the hymen:

  • Stage 0: No prolapse - all structures >1cm above hymen
  • Stage 1: Most dependent part >1cm above hymen (does not reach hymen)
  • Stage 2: Most dependent part ≤1cm above or below hymen (at or near introitus)
  • Stage 3: Most dependent part >1cm below hymen but <2cm less than total vaginal length (beyond introitus but not complete)
  • Stage 4: Complete vaginal eversion - most dependent part ≥ total vaginal length - 2cm (complete procidentia)

This Patient:

  • Uterine prolapse Stage 3 - cervix descends 2cm beyond hymen on straining (significant prolapse)
  • Multicompartment prolapse - anterior (cystocele) + apical (uterine) + posterior (rectocele)

Pathophysiology:

POP results from weakness/damage to pelvic floor support structures:

  • Levator ani muscle complex (main pelvic floor support)
  • Endopelvic fascia (connective tissue - cardinal/uterosacral ligaments)
  • Perineal body

Risk Factors Present in This Patient:

Vaginal deliveries (Para 4) Stretching/tearing of pelvic floor muscles, nerve damage (pudendal), fascial disruption ✓ YES - 4 vaginal deliveries

Instrumental deliveries (2 forceps) Increased pelvic floor trauma ✓ YES

Chronic constipation (lifelong) Repeated straining → chronic ↑ intra-abdominal pressure → progressive pelvic floor weakening ✓ YES - major contributing factor

Symptoms Explained:

  • Sensation of bulge/dragging: Uterine prolapse (Stage 3 - cervix beyond hymen)
  • Difficulty with bowel emptying + need for vaginal splinting: Rectocele (posterior wall prolapse traps stool)
  • Urinary symptoms (difficulty initiating, incomplete emptying): Cystocele (anterior wall prolapse) + elevated post-void residual (110mL)
  • Worse at end of day/standing: Gravity worsens prolapse
  • Improves lying down: Gravity relief

2. Outline a management plan for this patient, including conservative, mechanical, and surgical options. What factors would influence your choice of treatment?

Management of Pelvic Organ Prolapse (NICE 2021 / RCOG Guidelines)

Management Depends on:

  • Severity of prolapse (POP-Q stage)
  • Severity of symptoms (impact on quality of life)
  • Patient preference
  • Fitness for surgery (age, comorbidities)
  • Desire for future fertility (not relevant in this 63-year-old)

Step 1: Conservative Management (FIRST-LINE for ALL Patients)

A. Lifestyle Modifications (Reduce Intra-Abdominal Pressure):

  • Treat chronic constipation: CRITICAL in this patient (lifelong constipation = major contributing factor)
    • Dietary fibre (increase to 30g/day), adequate fluids (2L/day)
    • Laxatives: Continue lactulose, consider adding senna if inadequate response
    • Avoid straining at stool
  • Weight loss: Target BMI <30 (current BMI 31) - reduces intra-abdominal pressure
  • Avoid heavy lifting: Educate on correct lifting technique, avoid heavy loads
  • Chronic cough: Treat if present (not relevant in this case)

B. Pelvic Floor Muscle Training (PFMT):

  • Evidence: Can improve symptoms in Stage 1-2 prolapse, limited benefit in Stage 3-4 (like this patient)
  • Programme: Supervised by women's health physiotherapist - 8 contractions TDS for ≥3 months
  • Realistic expectations: Unlikely to reverse Stage 3 prolapse, but may slow progression and improve symptoms (pelvic pressure, urinary control)

C. Vaginal Oestrogen (Postmenopausal Women):

  • Indication: All postmenopausal women with POP (this patient is 12 years postmenopausal)
  • Options: Vaginal oestradiol cream (e.g., Ovestin), pessary (Vagifem), or ring (Estring)
  • Mechanism: Improves vaginal tissue quality, reduces atrophy, improves epithelial support
  • Note: Does NOT reverse prolapse but improves tissue integrity (essential if considering pessary or surgery)

Step 2: Mechanical Management - Vaginal Pessaries

Indications for Pessary:

  • Symptomatic prolapse (Stage 2-4) where patient declines or is unfit for surgery
  • Elderly/frail patients (not good surgical candidates)
  • Temporary measure while awaiting surgery
  • Many women choose pessary as first-line (avoids surgery, reversible)

Types of Pessaries:

  • Ring pessary (most common):
    • Sits in vaginal fornices, supports prolapse
    • Success rate 50-70% (patient can remove/reinsert themselves for intercourse)
    • Best for Stage 2-3 prolapse with good pelvic floor tone
  • Shelf pessary (Gellhorn):
    • Larger support platform - for severe/recurrent prolapse
    • Must be removed by clinician (cannot self-manage) - less convenient
    • Better for Stage 3-4 prolapse or poor pelvic floor tone

Pessary Management:

  • Fitting: Trial different sizes/types to find best fit (usually specialist nurse/urogynaecology clinic)
  • Review: Every 4-6 months (earlier if problems) - check for erosions, ulceration
  • Complications: Vaginal erosion (5-10%), discharge, bleeding, difficulty with intercourse, pessary expulsion
  • Contraindications: Active vaginal infection, vaginal bleeding (until cause identified), severe atrophy (treat with oestrogen first)

Step 3: Surgical Management (If Conservative/Pessary Fails or Patient Preference)

Indications for Surgery:

  • Symptomatic prolapse (Stage 2-4) with failed conservative management
  • Patient preference for definitive treatment
  • Pessary failure or intolerance
  • Significant impact on quality of life

Surgical Options:

A. Vaginal Hysterectomy + Pelvic Floor Repair (GOLD STANDARD for Uterine Prolapse):

  • Procedure: Remove uterus vaginally + repair anterior/posterior vaginal walls (anterior colporrhaphy for cystocele, posterior colporrhaphy for rectocele)
  • Advantages: Definitive treatment, no abdominal incisions, shorter recovery than abdominal hysterectomy
  • Success rate: 70-90% at 5 years (but 30-40% recurrence rate long-term)
  • Suitable for this patient: YES - multicompartment prolapse, age 63 (no future fertility), Stage 3

B. Vaginal Hysterectomy + Sacrospinous Fixation:

  • Addition to vaginal hysterectomy: Suture vaginal vault to sacrospinous ligament (provides apical support, reduces recurrence risk)
  • Advantage: Lower recurrence rate than hysterectomy alone

C. Uterine-Preserving Surgery (Manchester Repair):

  • Procedure: Cervical amputation + cardinal/uterosacral ligament plication (shortens ligaments to elevate uterus)
  • Indication: Young women desiring uterine preservation (not relevant in this 63-year-old)
  • Disadvantage: Higher recurrence rate than hysterectomy

D. Sacrohysteropexy (Laparoscopic/Robotic):

  • Procedure: Mesh attached to cervix/uterus, sutured to sacral promontory (suspends uterus)
  • Advantages: Preserves uterus, lower recurrence rate (10-15% at 5 years)
  • Disadvantages: Requires laparoscopy, mesh complications (erosion, chronic pain), expensive
  • Indication: Younger women desiring uterine preservation

E. Colpocleisis (Obliterative Surgery):

  • Procedure: Close vagina (suture anterior + posterior vaginal walls together) - NOT suitable for sexually active women
  • Advantages: Short procedure (30 minutes), low complication rate, very low recurrence (\<5%), suitable for elderly/frail
  • Disadvantages: Permanent loss of vaginal function (no intercourse), irreversible
  • Indication: Elderly/frail patients unfit for major surgery, not sexually active

Surgical Complications (ALL Procedures):

  • Bleeding, infection, urinary retention, bladder/bowel injury
  • Recurrence: 20-40% at 10 years (depends on procedure, risk factors)
  • De novo stress incontinence: 5-20% (previously "masked" by prolapse - prolapse kinks urethra, preventing leakage)
  • Dyspareunia: Vaginal narrowing/shortening

Management Plan for This Patient:

  1. Immediate (Conservative - FIRST-LINE):
    • Lifestyle: Treat constipation aggressively (optimize lactulose, add senna, dietary advice), weight loss referral (BMI 31 → target <30), avoid heavy lifting
    • Prescribe vaginal oestrogen (e.g., Ovestin cream) - essential for tissue integrity
    • Refer to women's health physiotherapy for supervised PFMT (may improve symptoms even if Stage 3)
  2. Review in 3 months: Assess response to conservative measures. If inadequate improvement:
    • Discuss options: Pessary vs surgery
    • If patient prefers non-surgical: Refer to urogynaecology for pessary fitting (likely ring or Gellhorn pessary for Stage 3 prolapse)
    • If patient prefers definitive treatment: Refer to urogynaecology for surgical assessment (likely vaginal hysterectomy + anterior/posterior repair ± sacrospinous fixation)
  3. Pre-operative optimization (if surgery chosen):
    • Continue vaginal oestrogen (improves surgical outcomes)
    • Optimize constipation management (prevent post-operative straining)
    • Weight loss (reduces surgical risk, improves outcomes)

Factors Influencing Treatment Choice in This Patient:

  • Age 63, postmenopausal: Uterine preservation not required → vaginal hysterectomy appropriate
  • Stage 3 prolapse: PFMT alone unlikely to be sufficient (but still worth trying), pessary or surgery likely needed
  • Multicompartment prolapse: Requires comprehensive repair (anterior + posterior + apical)
  • Significant symptoms (quality of life impact): Justifies surgical intervention if conservative fails
  • Chronic constipation (major risk factor): MUST be optimized before surgery (to prevent recurrence)
  • Fit for surgery (no major comorbidities mentioned): Good surgical candidate

Key Learning Points

  • Pelvic organ prolapse (POP) = descent of pelvic organs (uterus, bladder, rectum) from normal position, causing vaginal bulge - affects 50% of parous women (often asymptomatic)
  • Types: Anterior (cystocele - bladder), apical (uterine/vault), posterior (rectocele - rectum), enterocele (small bowel)
  • POP-Q staging: Stage 1 (above hymen) → Stage 4 (complete eversion) - based on descent relative to hymen
  • Risk factors: Vaginal deliveries (especially multiple, large babies, instrumental), menopause, obesity, chronic constipation, chronic cough, heavy lifting, age
  • Symptoms: Vaginal bulge (key symptom), pelvic pressure/dragging, urinary symptoms (cystocele), bowel symptoms (rectocele - need for vaginal splinting), worse at end of day/standing
  • First-line management: Conservative - treat constipation (CRITICAL), weight loss, PFMT (≥3 months), vaginal oestrogen (postmenopausal)
  • Pessaries (ring or Gellhorn): 50-70% success, reversible, review every 4-6 months - good option for elderly/frail or patient preference
  • Surgery (if conservative fails):
    • Vaginal hysterectomy + anterior/posterior repair: Gold standard for uterine prolapse (70-90% success)
    • Sacrospinous fixation: Add to hysterectomy for apical support (reduces recurrence)
    • Colpocleisis: Obliterative surgery for elderly/frail (closes vagina - NOT for sexually active women)
  • Surgical complications: Recurrence (20-40% at 10 years), de novo stress incontinence (5-20%), dyspareunia, bleeding, infection
  • Pre-operative optimization: Vaginal oestrogen, optimize constipation, weight loss
