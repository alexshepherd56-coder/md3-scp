Case 29.4 – Perimenopause & Menopause

Category: Medicine | Discipline: Obstetrics & Gynaecology | Setting: General Practice

Clinical Scenario

Mrs. Helen Clarke, a 49-year-old woman, presents to her GP with a 10-month history of irregular periods, hot flushes, and mood changes.

"Doctor, my periods have been all over the place for the past year. I'll go 2-3 months without a period, then have two periods in one month. I'm also getting terrible hot flushes - I wake up drenched in sweat 3-4 times a night, and I get them during the day too. I'm exhausted, irritable, and my concentration at work is terrible. I've also noticed my skin is really dry, and sex is uncomfortable because of vaginal dryness. My mum had severe osteoporosis, so I'm worried about that too."

History

  • Presenting complaint: Irregular periods (10 months), hot flushes (day and night), night sweats, vaginal dryness/dyspareunia
  • Menstrual history:
    • Previously regular 28-day cycle (menarche age 13)
    • Now irregular - cycles ranging 21-90 days, variable flow (sometimes heavy, sometimes light)
    • Last menstrual period: 6 weeks ago
  • Associated symptoms:
    • Vasomotor symptoms: Hot flushes 6-8 times/day, night sweats 3-4 times/night (disrupting sleep)
    • Psychological: Low mood, irritability, poor concentration, anxiety
    • Urogenital: Vaginal dryness, dyspareunia (painful intercourse)
    • Other: Joint aches, dry skin, reduced libido
    • No abnormal vaginal bleeding (no postcoital bleeding, no intermenstrual bleeding)
  • Obstetric/gynaecological history: Para 2 (2 vaginal deliveries at ages 28 and 31, no complications), no history of cervical abnormalities, cervical screening up-to-date (normal)
  • Past medical history: Migraines without aura (infrequent), hypothyroidism (well-controlled on levothyroxine)
  • Medications: Levothyroxine 75mcg OD, ibuprofen PRN (for joint aches)
  • Family history: Mother had severe osteoporosis (hip fracture age 68), no family history of breast cancer or VTE
  • Social history: Non-smoker, drinks 8 units alcohol/week, BMI 24, works as an accountant, sexually active with husband

Examination

  • General examination: Well, no signs of thyroid disease, BP 128/78 mmHg, BMI 24
  • Abdominal examination: Soft, non-tender, no masses
  • Pelvic examination (if indicated): Deferred (not required for perimenopause diagnosis in this age group with typical symptoms)

Investigations

  • FSH/LH: NOT routinely required for diagnosis in women >45 years with typical symptoms (NICE 2015)
  • TSH: 2.1 mIU/L (normal 0.4-4.0) - hypothyroidism well-controlled
  • Full blood count: Normal (excluding anaemia from heavy periods)

Questions

1. What is the diagnosis? Explain the definitions of perimenopause and menopause, and describe the clinical features that support this diagnosis.

Diagnosis: Perimenopause (Menopausal Transition)

Definitions (NICE 2015 / IMS Guidelines):

  • Perimenopause (menopausal transition): Period of time before menopause when ovarian function declines → irregular menstrual cycles + menopausal symptoms (vasomotor, psychological, urogenital). Typically lasts 4-8 years (average 4 years).
  • Menopause: Permanent cessation of menstruation - diagnosed retrospectively after 12 consecutive months of amenorrhoea (in the absence of other pathology). Average age in UK: 51 years (range 45-55).
  • Premature ovarian insufficiency (POI): Menopause before age 40 (1% of women)
  • Early menopause: Menopause age 40-45 (5% of women)
  • Postmenopause: Period after menopause (\>12 months since last period)

Why This is Perimenopause (Not Menopause Yet):

  • ✓ Age 49 (typical age for perimenopause - usually starts age 45-50)
  • ✓ Irregular periods for 10 months (classic perimenopause feature - NOT 12 months amenorrhoea)
  • ✓ Last period 6 weeks ago (still having periods, albeit irregular)
  • ✓ Classic menopausal symptoms (vasomotor, urogenital, psychological)

Pathophysiology:

Perimenopause/menopause is caused by ovarian follicular depletion:

  • Declining oestrogen production: Fewer ovarian follicles → ↓ oestradiol (E2) production
  • Loss of negative feedback: ↓ Oestrogen → ↑ FSH and LH (attempting to stimulate ovaries)
  • Irregular ovulation: Erratic follicle development → irregular/absent ovulation → irregular menstrual cycles
  • Consequences of oestrogen deficiency:
    • Vasomotor symptoms: Oestrogen withdrawal → thermoregulatory dysfunction (hypothalamus) → hot flushes, night sweats
    • Urogenital atrophy: Oestrogen receptors in vagina/urethra/bladder → atrophy, dryness, dyspareunia, urinary symptoms
    • Psychological: Oestrogen affects neurotransmitters (serotonin, noradrenaline) → mood changes, irritability, poor concentration
    • Long-term consequences: Bone loss (↑ osteoporosis risk), cardiovascular disease (loss of oestrogen's cardioprotective effect)

Clinical Features Supporting This Diagnosis:

A. Menstrual Changes (KEY FEATURE of Perimenopause):

  • ✓ Irregular periods for 10 months (cycles 21-90 days) - classic perimenopause
  • ✓ Variable flow (heavy then light) - erratic oestrogen/progesterone levels
  • ✓ Still having periods (last period 6 weeks ago) - NOT yet menopause

B. Vasomotor Symptoms (Most Common Menopausal Symptom - Affects 75%):

  • ✓ Hot flushes 6-8 times/day
  • ✓ Night sweats 3-4 times/night (disrupting sleep)
  • Duration: Seconds to minutes, sudden sensation of intense heat (face, neck, chest), flushing, sweating, followed by chills
  • Typical duration: Average 7-9 years (can persist >10 years in 10-15% of women)

C. Urogenital Symptoms (Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause - GSM):

  • ✓ Vaginal dryness, dyspareunia (painful intercourse)
  • ✓ Reduced libido
  • Also: Vaginal irritation/itching, urinary urgency/frequency (not mentioned in this case but common)

D. Psychological Symptoms:

  • ✓ Low mood, irritability, anxiety, poor concentration
  • ✓ Sleep disturbance (due to night sweats)
  • Note: Difficult to attribute entirely to menopause (life stressors, sleep deprivation also contribute)

E. Other Symptoms:

  • ✓ Joint aches (arthralgia - common in perimenopause)
  • ✓ Dry skin
  • Also common: Hair thinning, weight gain (especially central adiposity), palpitations, headaches

Diagnosis of Perimenopause (NICE 2015 Guidelines):

  • Women >45 years with typical symptoms: Diagnosis is CLINICAL - no need for FSH/LH testing
    • Irregular periods + menopausal symptoms = perimenopause
    • 12 months amenorrhoea = menopause
  • Women 40-45 years: Consider FSH testing (if symptoms atypical or uncertainty)
  • Women <40 years: FSH testing required (diagnose premature ovarian insufficiency - POI)
  • FSH levels (if tested):
    • FSH >30 IU/L on 2 occasions (4-6 weeks apart) = menopause/POI
    • FSH fluctuates in perimenopause → single measurement unreliable

Differential Diagnoses to Exclude:

  • Thyroid disease: Excluded (TSH normal, well-controlled hypothyroidism)
  • Pregnancy: Should be excluded in women with amenorrhoea (not relevant here - had period 6 weeks ago, but good practice)
  • Endometrial pathology: Heavy/irregular bleeding warrants investigation if concerning features (excluded by history - no postcoital/intermenstrual bleeding)

2. Outline a comprehensive management plan for this patient, including non-hormonal and hormonal options. Discuss the benefits and risks of HRT in this patient.

Management of Perimenopause/Menopausal Symptoms (NICE 2015 Guidelines)

Step 1: Education and Lifestyle Measures (FIRST-LINE for ALL Patients)

A. Education and Reassurance:

  • Explain perimenopause is a normal physiological process (not a disease)
  • Average duration of symptoms: 4-8 years (vasomotor symptoms average 7-9 years)
  • Contraception still required until 12 months after last period (age >50) or 24 months (age <50) - fertility declines but pregnancy still possible
  • Provide written information (e.g., Women's Health Concern leaflets, NICE patient information)

B. Lifestyle Modifications:

  • For vasomotor symptoms (hot flushes):
    • Wear layers, use fans, keep bedroom cool
    • Avoid triggers: Spicy food, caffeine, alcohol, smoking, stress
    • Regular exercise (moderate intensity, 150 min/week)
  • For bone health (family history of osteoporosis):
    • Weight-bearing exercise (walking, jogging, resistance training)
    • Calcium 700-1200mg/day (diet + supplements if needed)
    • Vitamin D 10mcg/day (400 IU) - all UK adults recommended
    • Avoid smoking, limit alcohol (\<14 units/week)
  • For mood/sleep:
    • Sleep hygiene (regular sleep schedule, avoid screens before bed)
    • Stress reduction (mindfulness, CBT, relaxation techniques)
    • Regular exercise (improves mood and sleep)
  • For vaginal dryness:
    • Vaginal moisturizers (e.g., Replens, Sylk) - used regularly 2-3 times/week
    • Lubricants for intercourse (water-based, e.g., KY Jelly, Durex)

Step 2: Hormonal Treatment - Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT)

Indications for HRT:

  • Vasomotor symptoms (hot flushes, night sweats) impacting quality of life - MOST EFFECTIVE TREATMENT
  • Urogenital symptoms (vaginal dryness, dyspareunia) - can use systemic HRT or local vaginal oestrogen
  • Prevention of osteoporosis (women with premature menopause <40 years, or high fracture risk)

This Patient is a GOOD CANDIDATE for HRT:

  • ✓ Age 49 (no age limit for starting HRT, but ideally start <60 years or within 10 years of menopause)
  • ✓ Moderate-to-severe vasomotor symptoms (hot flushes 6-8/day, night sweats disrupting sleep)
  • ✓ Urogenital symptoms (vaginal dryness, dyspareunia)
  • ✓ Family history of osteoporosis (mother had severe osteoporosis) - HRT protective
  • ✓ No contraindications (see below)

Types of HRT:

A. Systemic HRT (For Vasomotor Symptoms):

1. Combined HRT (Oestrogen + Progestogen) - For Women With Intact Uterus:

  • Why combined? Oestrogen alone causes endometrial hyperplasia/cancer → progestogen protects endometrium
  • Options:
    • Sequential (cyclical) HRT: Oestrogen daily + progestogen 12-14 days/month → withdrawal bleed
      • Indication: Perimenopausal women still having periods (THIS PATIENT)
      • Example: Femoston 1/10 (estradiol 1mg + dydrogesterone 10mg sequential)
    • Continuous combined HRT: Oestrogen + progestogen daily (no withdrawal bleed)
      • Indication: Postmenopausal women (\>12 months since last period)
      • Example: Femoston-conti (estradiol 0.5mg/1mg + dydrogesterone 2.5mg/5mg)
      • Note: Risk of irregular bleeding if started too early (within 1 year of last period)
  • Routes of administration:
    • Oral tablets: Most common, convenient
    • Transdermal patches: Applied twice weekly, avoid first-pass liver metabolism → lower VTE risk than oral (preferred if BMI >30, migraine, VTE risk factors)
    • Transdermal gel: Applied daily, flexible dosing

2. Oestrogen-Only HRT - For Women Without Uterus (Post-Hysterectomy):

  • Not relevant in this patient (has intact uterus)
  • No progestogen required → no withdrawal bleeds, lower breast cancer risk than combined HRT

B. Local Vaginal Oestrogen (For Urogenital Symptoms):

  • Indication: Vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, recurrent UTIs (genitourinary syndrome of menopause)
  • Options: Vaginal oestradiol cream (Ovestin), pessary (Vagifem), or ring (Estring)
  • Advantages: Minimal systemic absorption → does NOT require progestogen (safe for women with uterus), no increased VTE/stroke risk
  • Can be used alongside systemic HRT if urogenital symptoms persist
  • This patient: Could use vaginal oestrogen for dyspareunia (in addition to or instead of systemic HRT)

Benefits of HRT (Evidence-Based):

Benefit Evidence
Vasomotor symptoms 75% reduction in hot flushes/night sweats - most effective treatment
Urogenital symptoms Effective for vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, urinary urgency
Bone protection 50% reduction in osteoporotic fractures (hip, vertebral, wrist) - highly relevant for this patient (family history)
Colorectal cancer 30% reduction in colorectal cancer (combined HRT)
Quality of life Improves sleep, mood, concentration, sexual function
Cardiovascular disease Cardioprotective if started <60 years or within 10 years of menopause (this patient age 49 - good timing)

Contraindications to HRT (Absolute):

  • Current/previous breast cancer (oestrogen-sensitive)
  • Undiagnosed vaginal bleeding (exclude malignancy first)
  • Active VTE (DVT/PE) - can use HRT after anticoagulation stabilized (consider transdermal)
  • Active liver disease
  • Pregnancy

This Patient - Risk Assessment:

  • ✓ No family history breast cancer (low risk)
  • ✓ No family history VTE (low risk)
  • ✓ Non-smoker, BMI 24 (low VTE/CVD risk)
  • ✓ Age 49 (ideal age to start HRT - <60 and within 10 years menopause)
  • ✓ Migraines without aura: HRT safe (if migraines with aura → avoid oral HRT, use transdermal)
  • ✓ Family history osteoporosis: HRT HIGHLY BENEFICIAL (50% fracture reduction)

Step 3: Non-Hormonal Alternatives (If HRT Declined/Contraindicated)

For Vasomotor Symptoms:

  • SSRIs (e.g., fluoxetine 20mg, citalopram 10-20mg): 50% reduction in hot flushes (less effective than HRT but helpful)
  • SNRIs (e.g., venlafaxine 37.5-75mg): Similar efficacy to SSRIs
  • Clonidine (alpha-agonist): Modest benefit, side effects (dry mouth, dizziness) - third-line
  • CBT (Cognitive Behavioural Therapy): Helps coping with hot flushes, improves quality of life

For Vaginal Dryness (If Systemic HRT Declined):

  • Local vaginal oestrogen (first-line) - highly effective, minimal systemic absorption
  • Vaginal moisturizers/lubricants (less effective than oestrogen but safe)

Management Plan for This Patient:

  1. Immediate:
    • Education: Explain perimenopause, reassure, discuss symptom duration, contraception advice
    • Lifestyle: Avoid hot flush triggers, ensure adequate calcium/vitamin D (osteoporosis prevention), regular exercise
  2. HRT Recommendation (Age 49, Severe Symptoms, No Contraindications):
    • First-line: Sequential combined HRT (still having periods)
      • Oral option: Femoston 1/10 (estradiol 1mg + dydrogesterone 10mg sequential) - tablet once daily
      • Transdermal option: Evorel Sequi patches (if prefers patches, migraines, or VTE concerns)
    • Add vaginal oestrogen (e.g., Ovestin cream) if vaginal dryness/dyspareunia persists despite systemic HRT
    • Trial for 3 months (may take 2-3 months for full benefit)
  3. Review at 3 months:
    • Assess symptom improvement, side effects (breast tenderness, bloating, breakthrough bleeding - often settle after 3 months)
    • Adjust dose/type if needed
    • Check BP (if oral HRT)
  4. Annual review: Reassess symptoms, discuss duration of HRT, review risks/benefits, BP check, breast awareness
  5. Bone health: Consider DEXA scan (given family history of osteoporosis) - especially if stops HRT in future

Counselling Points for HRT:

  • Benefits outweigh risks for most women <60 years with menopausal symptoms (especially this patient - age 49, no contraindications)
  • Breast cancer risk: Small (4 extra cases per 1000 over 5 years) - lower than obesity/alcohol
  • Duration: No arbitrary limit - continue as long as benefits outweigh risks (review annually)
  • Transdermal preferred if VTE risk factors (not relevant here but good to know)
  • Osteoporosis protection: Major benefit for this patient (family history)

Key Learning Points

  • Perimenopause = menopausal transition before menopause, characterized by irregular periods + menopausal symptoms (lasts 4-8 years)
  • Menopause = 12 months amenorrhoea (permanent cessation of periods) - average age 51 years
  • Diagnosis in women >45 years: Clinical (irregular periods + symptoms) - NO need for FSH testing (NICE 2015)
  • Classic symptoms:
    • Vasomotor (75%): Hot flushes, night sweats (most common, average duration 7-9 years)
    • Urogenital: Vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, urinary urgency
    • Psychological: Low mood, irritability, poor concentration, sleep disturbance
  • First-line treatment: Lifestyle + HRT (if symptoms impacting quality of life)
  • HRT types:
    • Sequential combined: For perimenopausal women (still having periods) - oestrogen daily + progestogen 12-14 days/month
    • Continuous combined: For postmenopausal women (\>12 months since last period) - no withdrawal bleed
    • Local vaginal oestrogen: For urogenital symptoms (minimal systemic absorption, no progestogen needed)
  • HRT benefits: 75% reduction in hot flushes (most effective treatment), 50% reduction in osteoporotic fractures, improves QoL, cardioprotective if started <60 years
  • HRT risks: Breast cancer (+4 per 1000 over 5 years - combined HRT), VTE (+2 per 1000 - oral HRT only, transdermal safe), stroke (+1 per 1000, mainly >60 years)
  • Benefits outweigh risks for most women <60 years or within 10 years of menopause with menopausal symptoms
  • Transdermal HRT preferred if: VTE risk factors, BMI >30, migraines, hypertension (avoids first-pass metabolism)
  • Non-hormonal options: SSRIs/SNRIs (50% reduction in hot flushes), CBT, clonidine (less effective than HRT)
