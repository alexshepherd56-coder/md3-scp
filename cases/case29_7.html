Case 29.7 – Genital Herpes [SDL]

Category: Medicine | Discipline: Sexual Health | Setting: Self-Directed Learning

Learning Objectives

This is a self-directed learning (SDL) case. Use the key learning points below to guide your independent study of genital herpes.

Key Topics to Cover

  • Definition: Genital herpes = sexually transmitted infection caused by herpes simplex virus (HSV) - chronic, lifelong infection with latency and recurrence
  • Causative organisms:
    • HSV-2: Traditional "genital herpes" - 70% of genital herpes cases, more frequent recurrences
    • HSV-1: Traditional "oral herpes" (cold sores) - now accounts for 30% of genital herpes (due to oral sex), fewer recurrences
    • Cannot distinguish HSV-1 from HSV-2 clinically - requires lab testing
  • Epidemiology: Very common - 1 in 5 adults in UK seropositive for HSV-2 (most asymptomatic), incidence increasing (especially HSV-1 genital infections)
  • Transmission:
    • Sexual contact (genital-genital, oral-genital) - transmitted via skin-to-skin contact with infected lesions or asymptomatic viral shedding
    • Asymptomatic shedding (virus present on genital skin without lesions) - accounts for 70% of transmission
    • Vertical transmission (mother-to-neonate) - risk highest with primary infection in late pregnancy (30-50% transmission risk)
  • Clinical features:
    • Primary infection (first episode - MOST SEVERE):
      • Incubation: 2-12 days (average 4 days)
      • Symptoms: Multiple painful genital ulcers (vesicles → ulcers → crusts), severe pain, dysuria, inguinal lymphadenopathy, systemic symptoms (fever, malaise, myalgia)
      • Duration: 2-3 weeks (lesions heal without scarring)
      • Complications: Urinary retention (severe dysuria), aseptic meningitis (10-30% of primary infections), sacral radiculopathy
    • Recurrent infection (MILDER):
      • Trigger: Virus reactivates from dorsal root ganglia (latency) - triggered by stress, illness, UV exposure, immunosuppression
      • Symptoms: Fewer lesions (usually <5), less pain, no systemic symptoms
      • Duration: 7-10 days (shorter than primary)
      • Frequency: Variable - HSV-2 (median 4 recurrences/year, decreases over time), HSV-1 (fewer recurrences - median 1/year)
      • Prodrome: Tingling, itching, burning sensation 12-24 hours before lesions appear
    • Atypical presentations: Fissures, erythema, atypical ulcers (especially in immunosuppressed - larger, deeper, longer-lasting)
  • Diagnosis (BASHH 2014 Guidelines):
    • NAAT/PCR on lesion swab (GOLD STANDARD): Detects HSV DNA, type-specific (distinguishes HSV-1 vs HSV-2) - highest sensitivity
    • Viral culture: Less sensitive than PCR (especially in recurrent infection or healing lesions) - no longer recommended as first-line
    • HSV serology (IgG): Detects past infection (seroprevalence) - NOT useful for acute diagnosis (takes 12 weeks to seroconvert), limited clinical utility (cannot distinguish genital vs oral site, high background seroprevalence)
    • Swab technique: Unroof vesicle, swab base of ulcer vigorously - send in viral transport medium
  • Management (BASHH 2014 Guidelines):
    • Primary infection - Antiviral treatment (start immediately, ideally within 5 days of symptom onset):
      • Aciclovir 400mg TDS for 5 days (or valaciclovir 500mg BD for 5 days)
      • Effect: Reduces symptom duration, viral shedding, complications (does NOT cure infection or prevent recurrences)
      • Analgesia: Paracetamol, ibuprofen, topical lidocaine gel
      • Advise: Saline bathing, avoid sex until lesions healed, plenty of fluids (prevent urinary retention)
    • Recurrent infection:
      • Episodic treatment: Aciclovir 800mg TDS for 5 days (or valaciclovir 500mg BD for 3-5 days) - start at first sign of prodrome (most effective if started early)
      • Suppressive therapy (if ≥6 recurrences/year or severe psychological impact):
        • Aciclovir 400mg BD (or valaciclovir 500mg OD) - continuous daily dosing
        • Effect: Reduces recurrence frequency by 70-80%, reduces asymptomatic shedding (lowers transmission risk), improves quality of life
        • Duration: Review after 12 months (consider stopping to reassess recurrence frequency)
    • Partner management:
      • Offer partner screening (often asymptomatic)
      • Counsel on transmission risk: Asymptomatic shedding occurs, condoms reduce risk (50%), suppressive therapy reduces transmission risk (48%)
      • Avoid sex during outbreaks (highest transmission risk)
  • Pregnancy and neonatal herpes:
    • Neonatal herpes: Rare (1.65 per 100,000 live births in UK) but severe - mortality 30% (untreated), neurological sequelae 50%
    • Risk of transmission:
      • Primary infection in 3rd trimester (especially at delivery): 30-50% transmission risk (NO maternal antibodies to protect neonate)
      • Recurrent infection at delivery: \<3% transmission risk (maternal antibodies protective)
      • Established infection before pregnancy: Very low risk (\<1%)
    • Management (RCOG 2014 Guidelines):
      • Primary infection in 1st/2nd trimester: Aciclovir 400mg TDS (safe in pregnancy), vaginal delivery likely safe
      • Primary infection in 3rd trimester (≥28 weeks):
        • Aciclovir 400mg TDS until delivery
        • Offer elective caesarean section at 39 weeks (if primary infection within 6 weeks of delivery) - reduces neonatal herpes risk
      • Recurrent infection: Vaginal delivery safe (low transmission risk), offer suppressive aciclovir from 36 weeks (reduces recurrence at delivery)
      • Lesions at onset of labour: Offer caesarean section (reduces neonatal exposure)
  • Key counselling points:
    • Genital herpes is a chronic, lifelong infection (no cure) - virus remains latent in dorsal root ganglia
    • Recurrences are common but usually milder (frequency decreases over time)
    • Transmission can occur without visible lesions (asymptomatic shedding) - condoms reduce risk but do not eliminate it
    • Psychological impact: Stigma, anxiety, depression - offer counselling, support groups (Herpes Viruses Association)
    • Most people with HSV are asymptomatic (unaware they have infection) - normalizing diagnosis important
