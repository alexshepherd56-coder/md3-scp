Case 3.3 – Surgical Wound Management

Category: Surgery | Discipline: Surgery - General | Setting: Hospital

Case

Wishie Lika is an 81-year-old man with rheumatoid disease who is on prednisolone. He underwent an emergency laparotomy for ischaemic bowel. Four days post-operatively, his wound dehisced, revealing an 8cm opening with intestines visible through the wound.

Questions

1. What is a wound?

A wound is a disruption of the normal anatomic structure and function of tissue. Wounds can be classified in several ways:

By Mechanism of Injury:

  • Incised wounds: Clean cuts made by sharp objects (e.g., surgical incisions, knife cuts)
  • Lacerated wounds: Irregular tears in tissue caused by blunt trauma
  • Abrasions: Superficial wounds where the skin is scraped or rubbed off
  • Puncture wounds: Deep, narrow wounds caused by pointed objects
  • Contusions: Bruises caused by blunt trauma without breaking the skin
  • Burns: Tissue damage from heat, chemicals, electricity, or radiation

By Cleanliness/Contamination:

  • Clean: Non-traumatic, non-infected wounds (e.g., elective surgical incisions)
  • Clean-contaminated: Surgical wounds entering the GI, respiratory, or genitourinary tracts without significant spillage
  • Contaminated: Open traumatic wounds, major breaks in sterile technique, or gross spillage from GI tract
  • Dirty/Infected: Wounds with established infection or containing devitalized tissue

By Depth:

  • Superficial: Involving epidermis only
  • Partial thickness: Involving epidermis and dermis
  • Full thickness: Extending through all layers of skin into subcutaneous tissue or deeper
2. What are the phases of wound healing?

Wound healing is a complex, dynamic process that occurs in overlapping phases:

Phase Timing Key Events Cells Involved
Hemostasis Immediate (seconds to minutes)
  • Vasoconstriction
  • Platelet aggregation
  • Clot formation (fibrin mesh)
  • Release of growth factors
 Platelets, endothelial cells
Inflammatory Phase 0-3 days
  • Vasodilation and increased permeability
  • Neutrophil infiltration (phagocytosis of bacteria and debris)
  • Macrophage infiltration (debridement and growth factor release)
  • Clinical signs: erythema, warmth, swelling, pain
 Neutrophils, macrophages, lymphocytes
Proliferative Phase 3 days to 3 weeks
  • Angiogenesis (new blood vessel formation)
  • Granulation tissue formation
  • Collagen deposition by fibroblasts
  • Re-epithelialization
  • Wound contraction (myofibroblasts)
 Fibroblasts, endothelial cells, keratinocytes, myofibroblasts
Remodeling/Maturation Phase 3 weeks to 1-2 years
  • Collagen reorganization (Type III → Type I)
  • Increased tensile strength
  • Scar formation
  • Reduced vascularity
  • Maximum tensile strength: ~80% of original tissue
 Fibroblasts

Note: These phases overlap and are interdependent. Disruption at any stage can lead to impaired healing.

3. What is the role of granulation tissue?

Granulation tissue is the pink/red, moist, bumpy tissue that forms during the proliferative phase of wound healing. It plays several critical roles:

Composition:

  • New capillary networks (angiogenesis)
  • Fibroblasts producing collagen
  • Macrophages and inflammatory cells
  • Extracellular matrix (ECM) components
  • Growth factors and cytokines

Functions:

  • Fills the wound defect: Provides structural scaffold to fill the wound space from the base upward
  • Provides vascularity: New blood vessels deliver oxygen, nutrients, and immune cells to the healing tissue
  • Supports epithelialization: Provides a base for epithelial cells to migrate across
  • Fights infection: Increased blood flow brings immune cells and antibodies
  • Contracts the wound: Myofibroblasts in granulation tissue contract to reduce wound size
  • Foundation for scar tissue: Granulation tissue matures into scar tissue during remodeling

Clinical Assessment:

  • Healthy granulation tissue: Pink to red, moist, bumpy appearance, bleeds easily
  • Unhealthy granulation tissue: Dark red/purple, pale, or gray; may indicate infection, ischemia, or poor healing
  • Hypergranulation (proud flesh): Excessive granulation tissue rising above wound edges, impeding epithelialization
4. What factors are involved in wound healing?

Wound healing is influenced by both local and systemic factors:

Local Factors Systemic Factors
Oxygenation and Perfusion:
  • Adequate blood supply essential for oxygen and nutrient delivery
  • Ischemia impairs all phases of healing
  • Smoking reduces tissue oxygenation
Infection:
  • Bacterial contamination prolongs inflammation
  • Biofilm formation impedes healing
  • Increased tissue destruction
Foreign Bodies:
  • Sutures, dirt, necrotic tissue
  • Perpetuate inflammation
  • Increase infection risk
Mechanical Stress:
  • Excessive tension impairs healing
  • Movement disrupts forming tissue
  • Pressure causes tissue ischemia
Moisture Balance:
  • Moist environment optimal for healing
  • Desiccation slows epithelialization
  • Excessive moisture macerates tissue
Temperature:
  • Hypothermia impairs healing
  • Optimal temperature ~37°C
 Age:
  • Elderly: slower healing, thinner skin
  • Reduced cell proliferation
  • Decreased immune response
Nutrition:
  • Protein: Essential for collagen synthesis
  • Vitamin C: Collagen cross-linking
  • Vitamin A: Epithelialization, immune function
  • Zinc: Protein synthesis, cell proliferation
  • Malnutrition significantly impairs healing
Medications:
  • Steroids: Inhibit inflammation and collagen synthesis
  • NSAIDs: May impair early inflammation
  • Chemotherapy: Impairs cell proliferation
  • Immunosuppressants: Increase infection risk
Comorbidities:
  • Diabetes: Impaired immunity, neuropathy, vascular disease
  • Vascular disease: Reduced perfusion
  • Renal failure: Impaired protein metabolism
  • Immunocompromise: Infection risk, impaired healing
Other:
  • Smoking (vasoconstriction, reduced oxygenation)
  • Obesity (increased tension, reduced vascularity)
  • Stress (affects immune function)
5. What clinical factors in Wishie's history would impede wound healing and why?

Several factors in Wishie Lika's history significantly impede wound healing:

Factor Mechanism of Impairment
Advanced Age (81 years)
  • Reduced fibroblast activity and collagen synthesis
  • Decreased vascularity and tissue perfusion
  • Impaired immune response
  • Thinner, more fragile skin
  • Slower cell proliferation and migration
  • Reduced growth factor production
Prednisolone (Corticosteroid) Use
  • Anti-inflammatory effects: Suppresses the inflammatory phase, which is essential for wound healing initiation
  • Impaired collagen synthesis: Reduces fibroblast activity and collagen deposition
  • Reduced tensile strength: Wounds are weaker and more prone to dehiscence
  • Delayed epithelialization: Slower wound closure
  • Immunosuppression: Increased infection risk, reduced neutrophil and macrophage function
  • Impaired angiogenesis: Reduced new blood vessel formation
  • Protein catabolism: Reduces available amino acids for tissue repair
Rheumatoid Disease
  • Chronic inflammation: Dysregulated inflammatory response
  • Vasculitis: May compromise tissue perfusion
  • Nutritional deficiencies: Common in chronic disease
  • Immunosuppression: From disease and treatment
  • Poor tissue quality: Chronic inflammatory changes
Emergency Surgery for Ischaemic Bowel
  • Contamination: Bowel contents contaminating the surgical site increases infection risk
  • Poor tissue quality: Ischaemic tissue heals poorly
  • Emergency nature: Suboptimal surgical conditions, patient may be hemodynamically unstable
  • Physiological stress: Catabolic state impairs healing
  • Potential sepsis: Systemic infection diverts resources from healing
  • Malnutrition risk: Bowel pathology may indicate prior nutritional compromise
Abdominal Location
  • High mechanical stress from increased intra-abdominal pressure
  • Movement with breathing, coughing, mobilization
  • Risk of ileus and distension creating tension

Summary: Wishie has multiple overlapping risk factors (advanced age, steroids, chronic disease, contaminated emergency surgery) that synergistically impair all phases of wound healing, making him at very high risk for complications such as the wound dehiscence he experienced.

6. What factors govern whether absorbable or non-absorbable sutures are used?

The choice between absorbable and non-absorbable sutures depends on several factors:

Absorbable Sutures

Materials: Natural (catgut, chromic gut) or Synthetic (polyglactin/Vicryl, polyglycolic acid/Dexon, polydioxanone/PDS, poliglecaprone/Monocryl)

Indication Rationale
Internal tissues that heal quickly Subcutaneous tissue, muscle, mucosa - no need for permanent support
Tissues requiring temporary support Suture strength needed only until tissue gains adequate tensile strength
Areas where suture removal is difficult Deep tissues, internal organs, pediatric patients
Contaminated or infected wounds Non-absorbable sutures can act as nidus for infection
Rapidly healing tissues Mucosa, well-vascularized tissues

Considerations:

  • Absorption time varies: rapid (7-10 days) to extended (60-90 days)
  • Loss of tensile strength precedes complete absorption
  • Synthetic absorbables have more predictable absorption
  • Can cause inflammatory reaction during absorption

Non-Absorbable Sutures

Materials: Silk, nylon, polypropylene (Prolene), polyester, stainless steel

Indication Rationale
Skin closure Allows controlled removal after adequate healing time
Tissues under high tension Fascia, tendons - require prolonged or permanent support
Slowly healing tissues Fascia, tendons, aponeuroses
Cardiovascular surgery Vessel anastomoses, valve replacement - permanent support needed
Tissues that heal poorly Patients with impaired healing (steroids, diabetes, etc.)
When permanent support needed Hernia repairs, joint repairs

Considerations:

  • Require removal (except when buried)
  • May act as foreign body and nidus for infection if buried
  • Minimal tissue reaction
  • Maintain tensile strength indefinitely

Key Decision Factors:

Factor Absorbable Non-Absorbable
Tissue healing rate Fast healing Slow healing
Required support duration Temporary (weeks) Extended or permanent
Tension on wound Low to moderate High tension
Tissue type Muscle, subcutaneous, mucosa Skin, fascia, tendons
Accessibility Difficult to access for removal Easily accessible
Infection risk Contaminated wounds Clean wounds
7. What special steps might have been taken in wound closure to prevent dehiscence?

In high-risk patients like Wishie (elderly, on steroids, emergency surgery, contaminated field), several special techniques can reduce the risk of wound dehiscence:

Suture Material Selection:

  • Non-absorbable or delayed absorbable sutures for fascia: Polypropylene (Prolene), nylon, or long-acting absorbable (PDS) maintain strength longer
  • Strong suture material: Use larger gauge sutures (e.g., 1 or 0) for fascia
  • Monofilament preferred: Less tissue drag, lower infection risk than braided

Suture Techniques:

  • Mass closure technique: Single-layer closure of fascia and peritoneum together (rather than layered closure)
  • Continuous suture: Distributes tension more evenly than interrupted sutures
  • Small bites, short intervals: Sutures placed 5mm from wound edge, 5mm apart (reduces ischemia while maintaining strength)
  • Suture length to wound length ratio: Should be at least 4:1 (Jenkins rule) to prevent cutting through tissue
  • Avoid excessive tension: Sutures tight enough to approximate tissue but not strangle it
  • Full-thickness fascial bites: Ensure adequate tissue in each bite

Reinforcement Techniques:

  • Retention (tension) sutures: Large, deep sutures through all layers of abdominal wall placed lateral to incision, using heavy non-absorbable material with bolsters to prevent cutting through skin
  • Far-near-near-far technique: For high-risk closures
  • Smead-Jones closure: Figure-of-eight mass closure technique providing extra strength
  • Double-layer closure: In very high-risk cases, though mass closure often preferred

Surgical Technique:

  • Meticulous hemostasis: Reduce hematoma formation
  • Gentle tissue handling: Minimize tissue trauma and devitalization
  • Adequate wound debridement: Remove all necrotic and contaminated tissue
  • Copious irrigation: Reduce bacterial load
  • Layered closure: Close dead space to prevent seroma/hematoma
  • Avoid strangulation: Ensure sutures are not too tight, maintaining tissue perfusion

Alternative Closure Methods:

  • Delayed primary closure: In heavily contaminated wounds, leave wound open initially, close when infection risk reduced (3-5 days)
  • Mesh reinforcement: Prophylactic mesh in very high-risk patients (controversial)
  • Skin closure options:
    • Retention sutures with secondary wound care
    • Negative pressure wound therapy
    • Delayed skin closure

Postoperative Measures:

  • Abdominal binder: Provides external support, may reduce dehiscence risk
  • Minimize intra-abdominal pressure:
    • Prevent/treat ileus, constipation, urinary retention
    • Control coughing (incentive spirometry, adequate analgesia)
    • Avoid heavy lifting, straining
  • Nutritional support: Early feeding, protein supplementation
  • Optimization of medical conditions: Glycemic control, steroid management
  • Infection prevention: Antibiotics if indicated, aseptic technique

Special Considerations for Wishie:

  • Given steroid use: consider delaying steroid dose or temporarily increasing vitamin A supplementation (can counteract some steroid effects on healing)
  • Extended antibiotic coverage given contaminated field
  • Enhanced nutritional support
  • Possibly retention sutures given multiple risk factors
  • Close monitoring for signs of dehiscence
8. How would you manage Wishie's situation?

Wishie has experienced complete wound dehiscence (burst abdomen) with evisceration (intestines visible). This is a surgical emergency requiring immediate management:

Immediate Management (Emergency):

  1. Resuscitation and Stabilization:
    • Call for senior surgical help immediately
    • IV access with large bore cannulas
    • Fluid resuscitation (crystalloid)
    • Keep patient nil by mouth (NBM)
    • Oxygen therapy if needed
    • Analgesia (IV opioids)
    • Monitor vital signs closely
  2. Protect the Wound:
    • Do not attempt to replace bowel
    • Cover exposed bowel with sterile saline-soaked gauze
    • Cover with sterile towels/drape
    • Maintain moisture - prevent desiccation
    • Keep patient supine with knees flexed to reduce abdominal tension
  3. Investigations:
    • Full blood count (FBC) - check for anemia, infection
    • Urea and electrolytes (U&E) - assess renal function, electrolytes
    • Coagulation screen
    • Blood cultures if septic
    • Group and save/crossmatch blood
    • Arterial blood gas if hemodynamically unstable
    • ECG (elderly patient, pre-operative assessment)
    • Chest X-ray
  4. Infection Control:
    • Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics covering GI flora (e.g., co-amoxiclav or cephalosporin + metronidazole)
    • Wound swabs for culture and sensitivity
  5. Prepare for Theatre:
    • Consent for re-operation (repair of dehiscence +/- further bowel surgery)
    • Inform anesthetist and book emergency theatre
    • Insert nasogastric tube (decompress stomach)
    • Insert urinary catheter (monitor output, decompress bladder)
    • Inform intensive care unit - may need postoperative HDU/ITU care

Surgical Management:

  1. Re-exploration:
    • General anesthetic with full muscle relaxation
    • Examine bowel for viability, further ischemia, or perforation
    • Assess for intra-abdominal complications (abscess, anastomotic leak)
    • Remove any necrotic tissue
    • Copious irrigation with warm saline
  2. Wound Closure:
    • Primary closure if conditions favorable:
      • Clean wound after irrigation
      • Viable tissue
      • No significant infection
      • Use strong, non-absorbable sutures (e.g., Prolene 1 or 0)
      • Mass closure technique with continuous or interrupted sutures
      • Retention sutures strongly recommended: Heavy non-absorbable sutures (e.g., nylon 1) through all layers, placed 2-3cm lateral to wound edges, with bolsters
    • Alternative approaches if contaminated/infected:
      • Temporary abdominal closure (e.g., vacuum-assisted closure, Bogota bag)
      • Planned re-exploration and delayed closure
      • In severe cases, may need to leave wound open for secondary healing or later closure/grafting

Postoperative Management:

  • HDU/ITU care: Likely needed given age, comorbidities, major surgery
  • Antibiotics: Continue broad-spectrum, adjust based on cultures
  • Nutrition: Early enteral nutrition if possible, or parenteral nutrition
  • Analgesia: Adequate pain control (epidural or IV patient-controlled analgesia)
  • Respiratory care: Physiotherapy, incentive spirometry, early mobilization
  • VTE prophylaxis: Mechanical and pharmacological
  • Abdominal binder: Provide external support
  • Wound care: Regular inspection, aseptic dressing changes
  • Minimize abdominal pressure: Prevent constipation, ileus, coughing
  • Steroid management: Continue prednisolone (stopping acutely dangerous), consider stress dose steroid cover perioperatively
  • Optimize healing:
    • Glycemic control
    • Nutritional support (high protein)
    • Vitamin/mineral supplementation (vitamin C, zinc, vitamin A)
    • Treat anemia if present

Long-term Considerations:

  • High risk of incisional hernia development
  • May need planned hernia repair in future
  • Retention sutures typically removed after 2-3 weeks
  • Extended wound monitoring
9. If wound edges were necrotic and infected, how would management alter?

Necrotic and infected wound edges significantly complicate management and alter the surgical approach:

Altered Immediate Management:

  • More aggressive resuscitation: Sepsis likely, requiring aggressive fluid resuscitation and early antibiotics
  • Sepsis management: Follow sepsis protocol (Sepsis Six: oxygen, blood cultures, antibiotics, fluids, lactate, urine output monitoring)
  • Broader antibiotic coverage: May need extended spectrum (e.g., meropenem or piperacillin-tazobactam) to cover resistant organisms and anaerobes
  • Hemodynamic support: May need vasopressors if septic shock

Altered Surgical Management:

  1. Extensive Debridement (Essential):
    • Remove all necrotic tissue from wound edges
    • Excise back to healthy, bleeding tissue
    • May need to extend wound to ensure adequate debridement
    • Remove necrotic fascia, subcutaneous tissue, and skin
    • Ensure all tissue that will be closed is viable
    • Consider taking tissue samples for deep culture and histology
  2. Wound Irrigation:
    • Copious irrigation with warm saline (several liters)
    • May use pulsatile lavage
    • Consider antiseptic solutions in heavily contaminated wounds
  3. Modified Closure Strategy:
    • Primary closure generally NOT recommended if significant necrosis/infection
    • Preferred approaches:
      • Delayed primary closure: Leave wound open, dress with saline-soaked gauze, return to theatre in 48-72 hours when infection controlled and healthy granulation tissue forming
      • Negative pressure wound therapy (NPWT/VAC):
        • Applied after debridement
        • Protects bowel (cover with non-adherent layer first)
        • Reduces bacterial load
        • Promotes granulation tissue
        • Can be used as temporary abdominal closure
        • Changed every 48-72 hours with re-assessment
      • Temporary abdominal closure:
        • Bogota bag (sterile IV bag sutured to fascia)
        • Commercial temporary closure devices
        • Allows access for planned re-explorations
  4. Staged Approach:
    • First operation: Debridement, irrigation, temporary closure
    • Subsequent operations (every 24-72 hours): Further debridement as needed, assess for closure
    • Definitive closure: When wound clean, granulating, infection controlled

Closure Options Once Infection Controlled:

  • Delayed primary closure: If can be achieved within 3-5 days
  • Split-thickness skin grafting: If fascia closed but skin/subcutaneous defect too large
  • Component separation technique: For large defects
  • Mesh repair: Biological or synthetic mesh if fascial closure not possible
  • Planned ventral hernia: In extreme cases, close skin only, plan elective hernia repair later when patient stable
  • Secondary intention healing: Small wounds may be allowed to granulate

Enhanced Postoperative Management:

  • ICU care: Likely required for sepsis management
  • Prolonged antibiotics: Guided by cultures, typically 5-7 days or longer
  • Source control: Ensure no ongoing intra-abdominal sepsis
  • Serial lactates: Monitor response to sepsis treatment
  • Enhanced nutritional support: Increased protein requirements due to large wound
  • Regular wound inspection: Daily assessment for further necrosis, infection
  • Wound care:
    • Aseptic dressing changes
    • Specialized wound care team involvement
    • Documentation of wound progression (photographs, measurements)

Microbiological Considerations:

  • Send tissue (not just swabs) for deep culture
  • Look for specific organisms:
    • Anaerobes (Bacteroides, Clostridium)
    • Enterobacteriaceae
    • Enterococcus
    • MRSA in healthcare-associated infections
    • Fungi in immunocompromised
  • Adjust antibiotics based on culture results and sensitivities
  • Consider fungal coverage if prolonged antibiotics or immunosuppressed

Key Principles:

  1. Dead tissue will not heal: Aggressive debridement is essential
  2. Infected tissue will not hold sutures: Do not attempt primary closure
  3. Control infection first: Closure is secondary to controlling sepsis
  4. Multiple operations expected: Prepare patient and family for staged approach
  5. Accept temporary solutions: Temporary closure better than compromised primary closure
  6. Plan for definitive repair later: Once patient stable, wound clean, can plan reconstructive options

Prognosis with Necrosis and Infection:

  • Significantly higher morbidity and mortality
  • Prolonged hospital stay
  • Almost certain incisional hernia if survives
  • Risk of enterocutaneous fistula
  • Potential for chronic wound
  • In elderly patient like Wishie, mortality risk is substantial
