Case 29.6 – Vulvovaginal Candidiasis [SDL]

Category: Medicine | Discipline: Sexual Health & Gynaecology | Setting: Self-Directed Learning

Learning Objectives

This is a self-directed learning (SDL) case. Use the key learning points below to guide your independent study of vulvovaginal candidiasis.

Key Topics to Cover

  • Definition: Vulvovaginal candidiasis (VVC / "thrush") = fungal infection of vulva and vagina caused by Candida species - NOT a sexually transmitted infection
  • Causative organism: Candida albicans (85-90%), C. glabrata (5-10% - more common in recurrent VVC, resistant to azoles)
  • Epidemiology: 75% of women experience at least one episode in their lifetime, 40-45% have ≥2 episodes
  • Pathophysiology: Candida is normal vaginal commensal - overgrowth occurs when protective lactobacilli depleted or local immunity impaired
  • Risk factors:
    • Antibiotic use: Kills protective lactobacilli → allows Candida overgrowth (most common precipitant)
    • High oestrogen states: Pregnancy, COCP (high-dose), HRT - oestrogen promotes Candida growth
    • Immunosuppression: Diabetes (especially poorly controlled - high glucose), HIV, corticosteroids, chemotherapy
    • Other: Tight clothing, perfumed soaps/bubble baths, douching (disrupts vaginal flora)
  • Clinical features:
    • Vulval itching (pruritus) - KEY SYMPTOM - often intense
    • Vulval soreness, burning, irritation
    • Vaginal discharge: Thick, white, "cottage cheese" appearance, non-offensive odor (unlike BV/TV)
    • Dyspareunia (superficial - due to vulval inflammation)
    • External dysuria (stinging on urination - urine on inflamed vulva)
    • Examination: Vulval erythema, edema, fissuring, satellite lesions, thick white adherent plaques on vaginal walls
  • Diagnosis:
    • Clinical diagnosis (typical symptoms + examination findings) - investigations not routinely required
    • High vaginal swab (HVS): If diagnosis uncertain, recurrent VVC, or treatment failure
      • Microscopy: Spores/pseudohyphae (KOH wet mount or Gram stain)
      • Culture: Confirms Candida species (distinguish C. albicans vs C. glabrata)
      • Note: Candida can be normal commensal (30% of asymptomatic women) - positive swab without symptoms does NOT require treatment
    • pH testing: Normal vaginal pH (3.5-4.5) - pH >4.5 suggests BV or TV (not VVC)
  • Differential diagnosis:
    • Bacterial vaginosis: Thin grey discharge, fishy odor, pH >4.5, NO itching
    • Trichomoniasis: Frothy yellow-green discharge, offensive odor, pH >4.5, vulval itching, strawberry cervix
    • Atrophic vaginitis: Postmenopausal, vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, no discharge
    • Contact dermatitis: History of irritant (soap, perfume), eczema/psoriasis
  • Management (BASHH 2019 Guidelines):
    • Uncomplicated VVC (first-line options - equivalent efficacy):
      • Intravaginal imidazole (clotrimazole): Clotrimazole 500mg pessary single dose (or 200mg for 3 nights) - topical, minimal systemic absorption
      • Oral fluconazole 150mg single dose: Convenient (single oral dose), systemic - contraindicated in pregnancy
      • Topical imidazole cream (clotrimazole 2%): Applied to vulva BD for 7 days - for external symptoms
      • Combination: Pessary + topical cream (if severe vulval symptoms)
    • Pregnancy: Intravaginal imidazole ONLY (longer course - 7 days) - avoid oral fluconazole (teratogenic)
    • Partner notification/treatment: NOT required (Candida is not an STI)
    • Advise: Avoid irritants (perfumed products, douching), wear loose cotton underwear, avoid sex until symptoms resolve
  • Recurrent vulvovaginal candidiasis (RVVC): ≥4 episodes per year (affects 5-8% of women)
    • Investigations: HVS (confirm diagnosis, identify species), exclude diabetes (HbA1c/fasting glucose), consider HIV testing
    • Management (BASHH 2019):
      • Induction: Fluconazole 150mg every 3 days (total 3 doses over 1 week)
      • Maintenance: Fluconazole 150mg once weekly for 6 months (prevents recurrence in 90%)
      • If C. glabrata (azole-resistant): Intravaginal nystatin 100,000 units for 14 nights, or boric acid pessaries
    • Lifestyle: Optimize diabetes control, avoid unnecessary antibiotics, probiotics (limited evidence - may help)
  • Key points:
    • VVC is NOT an STI - partner treatment not required
    • Antibiotic use is the most common trigger (consider prophylactic antifungal if recurrent post-antibiotics)
    • Fluconazole contraindicated in pregnancy - use topical imidazole
    • Recurrent VVC requires investigation (exclude diabetes, HIV, C. glabrata) + long-term suppressive therapy
    • Positive swab without symptoms does NOT require treatment (Candida is normal commensal)
