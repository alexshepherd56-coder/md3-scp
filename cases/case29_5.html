Case 29.5 – Premature Ovarian Insufficiency [SDL]

Category: Medicine | Discipline: Obstetrics & Gynaecology | Setting: Self-Directed Learning

Learning Objectives

This is a self-directed learning (SDL) case. Use the key learning points below to guide your independent study of premature ovarian insufficiency.

Key Topics to Cover

  • Definition: Premature ovarian insufficiency (POI) = menopause before age 40 (affects 1% of women) - previously called "premature menopause" or "premature ovarian failure"
  • Early menopause: Menopause age 40-45 (affects 5% of women) - managed similarly to POI
  • Causes of POI:
    • Idiopathic (50-90%): No identifiable cause - most common
    • Genetic: Turner syndrome (45,XO), Fragile X premutation (FMR1 gene - 2-6% of POI), familial (family history of early menopause)
    • Autoimmune: Autoimmune polyglandular syndrome (associated with Addison's disease, hypothyroidism, T1DM), autoimmune oophoritis
    • Iatrogenic: Chemotherapy (alkylating agents, cyclophosphamide), radiotherapy (pelvic radiation), bilateral oophorectomy
    • Infections: Mumps oophoritis (rare)
  • Diagnosis (ALL women <40 years with amenorrhoea/oligomenorrhoea):
    • FSH >30 IU/L on 2 occasions (4-6 weeks apart) + amenorrhoea/oligomenorrhoea for ≥4 months
    • Oestradiol low (usually <100 pmol/L)
    • Exclude pregnancy first (βhCG)
    • Do NOT diagnose menopause in women <40 based on symptoms alone - FSH testing required
  • Investigations (identify underlying cause):
    • Karyotype: Exclude Turner syndrome (45,XO), Turner mosaicism (45,XO/46,XX)
    • FMR1 gene testing: Fragile X premutation (offer genetic counselling if positive - risk of fragile X syndrome in offspring)
    • Autoimmune screen: TFTs, 9am cortisol, anti-adrenal antibodies, anti-thyroid antibodies, glucose (exclude autoimmune polyglandular syndrome)
    • Pelvic USS: Assess ovarian morphology
  • Symptoms: Same as menopause - vasomotor symptoms (hot flushes, night sweats), vaginal dryness, low mood, reduced libido, amenorrhoea/oligomenorrhoea
  • Long-term health consequences (MORE SEVERE than menopause at normal age):
    • Osteoporosis: Prolonged oestrogen deficiency → ↑ bone loss → ↑ fracture risk (offer DEXA scan at diagnosis)
    • Cardiovascular disease: Loss of oestrogen's cardioprotective effect → ↑ CVD risk (relative risk 2-3x higher)
    • Cognitive decline: Possible ↑ risk of dementia (evidence conflicting)
    • Psychological impact: Infertility distress, loss of femininity, anxiety, depression
    • Infertility: Spontaneous pregnancy rare (5-10% conceive spontaneously after diagnosis) - most require egg donation IVF
  • Management (NICE 2015 / BMS Guidelines):
    • HRT (oestrogen + progestogen) - STRONGLY RECOMMENDED until age 51 (average age of natural menopause):
      • Indication: Replace physiological oestrogen (not just symptom control) - prevent osteoporosis, CVD
      • Duration: Continue until age 51 (minimum) - then reassess (no increased breast cancer risk before age 51)
      • Combined oral contraceptive pill (COCP): Alternative to HRT (provides contraception + oestrogen replacement) - suitable for young women
      • Transdermal HRT preferred if: BMI >30, VTE risk factors, migraines
    • Bone protection: DEXA scan at diagnosis, calcium 1000-1200mg/day, vitamin D 10mcg/day (400 IU), weight-bearing exercise
    • Cardiovascular risk reduction: Optimize CV risk factors (smoking cessation, healthy diet, exercise, manage BP/cholesterol)
    • Fertility counselling:
      • Spontaneous pregnancy possible (5-10%) but unpredictable - contraception still required if pregnancy not desired
      • Egg donation IVF = most effective treatment for infertility (success rate 40-50% per cycle)
      • Fertility preservation NOT possible (ovarian reserve already depleted at diagnosis)
      • Refer to specialist fertility clinic
    • Psychological support: Counselling, support groups (e.g., Daisy Network - UK POI charity), address fertility distress
  • Key counselling points:
    • HRT is ESSENTIAL (not optional) - protect bones and heart
    • No increased breast cancer risk with HRT before age 51 (replacing physiological oestrogen, not adding excess)
    • Fertility highly unlikely but not impossible - egg donation IVF main option
    • Long-term follow-up required: Monitor bone health, cardiovascular risk
