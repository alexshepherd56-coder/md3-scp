Case 29.1 – Urinary Stress Incontinence

Category: Medicine | Discipline: Urogynaecology | Setting: General Practice

Clinical Scenario

Mrs. Linda Evans, a 52-year-old postmenopausal woman, attends her GP with a 2-year history of involuntary urine leakage.

"I'm so embarrassed, doctor. I leak urine whenever I cough, sneeze, or exercise. It's gotten so bad I've stopped going to my Zumba classes. I have to wear pads every day now. It's really affecting my confidence."

History

  • Presenting complaint: Involuntary urine leakage with coughing, sneezing, laughing, and exercise for 2 years (gradually worsening)
  • Character: Small-to-moderate volume leakage, worse with physical exertion, no urgency, no nocturia
  • Associated symptoms: No dysuria, no haematuria, no frequency, no urgency, no feeling of incomplete emptying
  • Obstetric history: Para 3 (3 vaginal deliveries, largest baby 4.2kg, one forceps delivery, third-degree tear repaired)
  • Gynaecological history: Menopause aged 50, no HRT, no prolapse symptoms
  • Past medical history: Asthma (salbutamol inhaler), chronic cough (smoker)
  • Medications: Salbutamol inhaler PRN
  • Social history: Smokes 15 cigarettes/day (30 pack-years), BMI 32, works as a primary school teacher

Examination

  • Abdominal examination: Soft, non-tender, no palpable masses, no organomegaly
  • Pelvic examination:
    • Inspection: Atrophic vulvovaginal changes (postmenopausal), no visible prolapse at rest
    • Speculum: Mild anterior vaginal wall descent (cystocele) on Valsalva, no uterine prolapse, cervix healthy
    • Bimanual: Normal-sized anteverted uterus, no adnexal masses
    • Cough stress test (with full bladder): Positive - immediate small-volume urine leakage on coughing
  • Neurological examination (lower limbs): Normal power, reflexes, and sensation

Investigations

  • Urine dipstick: Negative for blood, protein, leucocytes, nitrites
  • Bladder diary (3 days): Daytime frequency 6-8 times, nocturia 0-1 times, no urgency episodes, leakage only with physical activity
  • Post-void residual volume (bladder scan): 25mL (normal <50mL)

Questions

1. What is the most likely diagnosis? Explain the pathophysiology of this condition and identify the key risk factors present in this patient.

Diagnosis: Stress Urinary Incontinence (SUI)

Definition:

Stress urinary incontinence = involuntary urine leakage on effort/physical exertion (coughing, sneezing, laughing, exercise) due to increased intra-abdominal pressure WITHOUT urgency.

Pathophysiology:

SUI occurs when intra-abdominal pressure exceeds urethral closure pressure, causing involuntary urine loss. Two main mechanisms:

  • 1. Urethral hypermobility (most common): Weakness of pelvic floor muscles → loss of urethral support → bladder neck and proximal urethra descend during straining → ineffective urethral closure
  • 2. Intrinsic sphincter deficiency (ISD): Direct damage to urethral sphincter mechanism → weak urethral closure even at rest

Risk Factors Present in This Patient:

Risk Factor Mechanism Present in This Case?
Vaginal deliveries (Para 3) Stretching/tearing of pelvic floor muscles and nerves (pudendal nerve damage) ✓ YES - 3 vaginal deliveries
Large baby (4.2kg) Increased risk of pelvic floor trauma ✓ YES
Instrumental delivery (forceps) Increased pelvic floor trauma ✓ YES
3rd-degree perineal tear Severe pelvic floor disruption ✓ YES
Postmenopausal (oestrogen deficiency) Loss of urethral mucosal seal, reduced urethral vascularity, tissue atrophy ✓ YES - menopause age 50
Obesity (BMI 32) Chronic increased intra-abdominal pressure ✓ YES
Chronic cough (smoker, asthma) Repeated raised intra-abdominal pressure ✓ YES
Age (52 years) Progressive collagen weakness, muscle atrophy ✓ YES

Why This is SUI (Not Urge Incontinence):

  • ✓ Leakage only with physical exertion (coughing, sneezing, exercise) - NO urgency
  • ✓ Positive cough stress test - immediate leakage on coughing
  • ✓ No frequency (6-8 times/day is normal), no nocturia, no urgency episodes on bladder diary
  • ✓ Multiple risk factors for pelvic floor weakness

2. Outline your initial management plan for this patient, including conservative measures and when you would consider surgical intervention.

Initial Management of Stress Urinary Incontinence (NICE 2019 Guidelines)

Step 1: Conservative Management (FIRST-LINE - Trial for 3 Months)

A. Lifestyle Modifications:

  • Weight loss: Target BMI <30 (current BMI 32) - reduces intra-abdominal pressure
    • Evidence: 5-10% weight loss improves SUI by 50%
  • Smoking cessation: Reduces chronic cough (chronic raised intra-abdominal pressure)
  • Caffeine reduction: Caffeine is a bladder irritant (though less relevant in SUI vs urgency)
  • Fluid advice: Aim 1.5-2L/day - avoid restriction (concentrated urine irritates bladder)
  • Treat chronic cough: Review asthma control, consider cough suppressants

B. Supervised Pelvic Floor Muscle Training (PFMT) - FIRST-LINE TREATMENT:

  • Referral to specialist women's health physiotherapist
  • Programme: At least 8 contractions, 3 times per day, for ≥3 months
  • Technique: Contract and lift pelvic floor muscles (as if stopping urine mid-flow), hold 10 seconds, relax 10 seconds
  • Evidence: 50-70% of women report improvement/cure with supervised PFMT
  • Adjuncts: Vaginal cones, biofeedback, electrical stimulation (if difficulty isolating pelvic floor muscles)

C. Vaginal Oestrogen (Postmenopausal Women):

  • Indication: Vaginal atrophy (present in this case)
  • Options: Vaginal oestradiol cream (e.g., Ovestin), pessary (e.g., Vagifem), or ring (Estring)
  • Mechanism: Restores urethral mucosal seal, improves vascularity, increases urethral tone
  • Note: Does NOT treat SUI directly but improves urethral function and facilitates PFMT

D. Containment Products (Interim Measure):

  • Absorbent pads (patient already using - reassure this is acceptable while awaiting PFMT)
  • Avoid relying on these as sole management

Step 2: Surgical Management (If Conservative Treatment Fails After 3-6 Months)

Indications for Surgery:

  • Failure of conservative treatment (inadequate improvement after ≥3 months supervised PFMT)
  • Patient prefers surgical management
  • Significant impact on quality of life
  • Prerequisite: Patient must be counselled on risks/benefits, complete bladder diary, exclude reversible causes

Surgical Options:

A. Mid-Urethral Sling (MUS) - GOLD STANDARD SURGICAL TREATMENT:

  • Retropubic (TVT - Tension-free Vaginal Tape):
    • Success rate 80-90% at 5 years
    • Sling placed under mid-urethra, passed retropubically
    • Higher risk bladder injury (4%) vs TOT
  • Transobturator (TOT - Transobturator Tape):
    • Success rate 75-85% at 5 years (slightly lower than TVT)
    • Sling passed through obturator foramen
    • Lower risk bladder injury vs TVT, but higher groin pain risk
  • Complications:
    • Bladder injury (3-5%), vaginal erosion (1-3%), voiding dysfunction (2-5%), de novo urgency (5-10%)
    • Mesh complications (rare but serious): Chronic pain, dyspareunia, erosion
    • MHRA warning 2018: Synthetic mesh should only be used when conservative options have failed, by specialist surgeons in designated centres

B. Autologous Fascial Sling (Alternative if Mesh Declined):

  • Uses patient's own rectus fascia as sling material
  • Avoids mesh complications but requires larger incision, longer recovery
  • Similar efficacy to synthetic mesh

C. Colposuspension (Burch Procedure):

  • Sutures placed in paravaginal fascia to bladder neck, attached to iliopectineal ligament
  • Can be open or laparoscopic
  • Similar efficacy to MUS but more invasive, longer recovery, higher prolapse risk
  • Now second-line (MUS preferred)

D. Urethral Bulking Agents (For Intrinsic Sphincter Deficiency):

  • Injection of bulking material (collagen, silicone) into urethral submucosa to increase urethral closure pressure
  • Less effective than MUS (40-60% improvement), requires repeat injections
  • Option for elderly/frail patients unsuitable for surgery

Management Plan for This Patient:

  1. Immediate: Refer to women's health physiotherapy for supervised PFMT (3-month trial)
  2. Prescribe vaginal oestrogen (e.g., Ovestin cream) for vaginal atrophy
  3. Lifestyle: Smoking cessation support, weight loss referral (dietician), optimize asthma control
  4. Review in 3 months: If inadequate response → refer to urogynaecology for consideration of surgical options (likely TVT/TOT if patient willing)

Key Learning Points

  • Stress urinary incontinence (SUI) = involuntary urine leakage on physical exertion (coughing, sneezing, exercise) WITHOUT urgency - due to pelvic floor weakness ± intrinsic sphincter deficiency
  • Risk factors: Vaginal deliveries (especially large babies, instrumental, perineal tears), obesity, menopause, chronic cough, age
  • Diagnosis is clinical: History + positive cough stress test on examination (immediate leakage on coughing with full bladder)
  • Differentiate from urge incontinence: SUI = leakage with exertion (no urgency), urge incontinence = leakage preceded by sudden urge to void
  • First-line treatment: Supervised pelvic floor muscle training (PFMT) for ≥3 months - 50-70% improve/cure
  • Lifestyle: Weight loss (if BMI >30), smoking cessation (chronic cough), treat constipation
  • Postmenopausal women: Add vaginal oestrogen to improve urethral function and vaginal atrophy
  • Surgery if conservative fails: Mid-urethral sling (TVT/TOT) = gold standard (80-90% success) - but MHRA warning re: mesh complications
  • Complications of mesh surgery: Chronic pain, erosion, dyspareunia - should only be used in specialist centres after conservative options exhausted
  • Red flags requiring urgent referral: Haematuria (exclude malignancy), pelvic mass, suspected fistula, failed previous surgery
