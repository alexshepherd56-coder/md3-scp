Case 18.1 – Menorrhagia

Category: Medicine | Discipline: Obstetrics & Gynaecology | Setting: GP Surgery

Case

Mrs. Sarah Hughes, a 38-year-old accountant and mother of two, presents to her GP with a 9-month history of increasingly heavy periods. She reports needing to change super-absorbent sanitary pads every 2 hours on the heaviest days (days 1-3), passing clots the size of 50p coins, and her periods lasting 7-8 days (previously 5 days). She has experienced flooding through to her clothes on two occasions at work, which she found embarrassing.

She feels tired all the time and has noticed she gets breathless climbing stairs at work. Her periods occur regularly every 28-30 days and she describes cramping lower abdominal pain on days 1-2, managed with ibuprofen. She denies intermenstrual bleeding, postcoital bleeding, or pelvic pain outside of menstruation. Her last cervical smear 18 months ago was normal.

Past medical history includes two previous vaginal deliveries (ages 8 and 5). She has no significant medical history and takes no regular medications. She is in a stable relationship with her husband and uses condoms for contraception. She has completed her family and does not wish for more children.

On Examination:
  • General: Appears well, not in distress
  • Vital signs: BP 118/74 mmHg, HR 88 bpm, Temperature 36.7°C
  • Pale conjunctivae noted
  • Abdominal examination: Soft, non-tender, no masses palpable. No organomegaly
  • Per speculum: Cervix appears healthy, no visible lesions, minimal discharge
  • Bimanual: Uterus feels bulky and mildly enlarged (approximately 10-12 week size), smooth, mobile, non-tender. No adnexal masses. Fornices clear

Investigations

Full Blood Count:
  • Hb: 98 g/L (normal 115-165 g/L for women) - Anaemia
  • MCV: 72 fL (normal 80-100 fL) - Microcytic
  • MCH: 24 pg (normal 27-32 pg) - Hypochromic
  • WCC: 7.2 × 10⁹/L - Normal
  • Platelets: 385 × 10⁹/L - Normal
Ferritin: 8 μg/L (normal 15-300 μg/L) - Low, consistent with iron deficiency anaemia

Questions

Question 1: How is menorrhagia defined, and what are the key causes? What is the PALM-COEIN classification?

Definition: Heavy Menstrual Bleeding (HMB) is excessive menstrual blood loss (\>80mL per cycle OR >7 days) which interferes with a woman's physical, emotional, social, and material quality of life.

Clinical indicators: Changing pads every 1-2 hours, clots >2.5cm, flooding, periods >7 days, anaemia symptoms.

PALM-COEIN Classification (FIGO system for causes):

  • P - Polyp: Endometrial polyps
  • A - Adenomyosis: Endometrial tissue in myometrium (HMB + dysmenorrhoea + boggy uterus)
  • L - Leiomyoma: Fibroids (most common structural cause, especially submucosal)
  • M - Malignancy: Endometrial cancer/hyperplasia (age >45, PMB, obesity, PCOS)
  • C - Coagulopathy: Von Willebrand disease, ITP (HMB since menarche, easy bruising)
  • O - Ovulatory dysfunction: PCOS, thyroid, hyperprolactinaemia (irregular cycles)
  • E - Endometrial: Primary endometrial disorder (diagnosis of exclusion)
  • I - Iatrogenic: Copper IUD, anticoagulants, antiplatelets
  • N - Not classified: Other rare causes

This patient: Bulky uterus suggests fibroids (L) or adenomyosis (A). Next step: Pelvic ultrasound.

Question 2: What investigations should be performed, and what are the key management options for menorrhagia?

Investigations:

  • FBC + ferritin - All women (detect/treat anaemia)
  • Pelvic ultrasound (transvaginal) - If palpable mass, enlarged uterus, or treatment failure
  • Endometrial biopsy - Age ≥45 OR <45 with persistent IMB/risk factors for endometrial cancer
  • Coagulation screen - If HMB since menarche, bleeding history
  • TFTs, hormones - If irregular cycles

Medical Management (NICE Guidelines):

  • FIRST-LINE: LNG-IUS (Mirena)
    • Reduces blood loss by 80-90%
    • Provides contraception for 5 years
    • Side effects: Irregular bleeding first 3-6 months
  • Tranexamic acid 1g TDS/QDS (days 1-4)
    • Antifibrinolytic - reduces blood loss 40-50%
    • Non-hormonal, taken only during periods
  • Mefenamic acid 500mg TDS (days 1-3)
    • NSAID - reduces blood loss 20-30%
    • Also treats dysmenorrhoea
  • COCP - Reduces blood loss 40-50%, regulates cycles, contraception
  • Cyclical progestogens (norethisterone) - Less effective (15-20%)

Surgical Options (if medical treatment fails):

  • Endometrial ablation - Family complete, normal cavity <11cm. 80-90% satisfaction, 40-60% amenorrhoeic
  • Uterine artery embolization (UAE) - For fibroids, minimally invasive
  • Myomectomy - Remove fibroids, preserve fertility
  • Hysterectomy - Definitive treatment, 100% cure

This patient's plan:

  1. Oral iron (ferrous sulfate 200mg BD)
  2. Pelvic ultrasound to assess for fibroids/adenomyosis
  3. If no large submucosal fibroids: LNG-IUS first-line
  4. If large fibroids >3cm distorting cavity: Refer to gynae (myomectomy/UAE/hysterectomy)

Key Learning Points

  • HMB = excessive menstrual loss interfering with quality of life (\>80mL, >7 days, pads every 1-2hrs, clots >2.5cm)
  • PALM-COEIN: PALM = structural (Polyp, Adenomyosis, Leiomyoma, Malignancy); COEIN = non-structural (Coagulopathy, Ovulatory, Endometrial, Iatrogenic, Not classified)
  • Fibroids (40-60% women by age 35) most common structural cause. Submucosal fibroids cause most bleeding
  • Investigations: FBC+ferritin (all), USS (enlarged uterus/mass), biopsy (age ≥45)
  • First-line: LNG-IUS (80-90% reduction), tranexamic acid (40-50%), mefenamic acid (20-30%)
  • Surgical: Endometrial ablation (family complete), myomectomy (preserve fertility), hysterectomy (definitive)
  • Iron deficiency anaemia common - treat with oral iron
  • Red flags: PMB, persistent IMB/PCB, abnormal cervix → 2WW referral
