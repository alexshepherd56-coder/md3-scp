Case 29.2 – Urinary Urge Incontinence (Overactive Bladder)

Category: Medicine | Discipline: Urogynaecology | Setting: General Practice

Clinical Scenario

Mrs. Margaret Thompson, a 68-year-old woman, presents to her GP with a 6-month history of urinary urgency and incontinence.

"Doctor, I keep getting these sudden, overwhelming urges to pass urine. I can't hold it - by the time I reach the toilet, I've already wet myself. It's happening 3-4 times a day now. I'm afraid to leave the house in case I have an accident. I'm up 4 times every night to go to the toilet too."

History

  • Presenting complaint: Sudden urge to urinate with inability to hold, leading to leakage before reaching the toilet (6 months, worsening)
  • Frequency: Daytime urinary frequency 12-15 times/day, nocturia 4 times/night
  • Character: Sudden, overwhelming urge with leakage not related to physical exertion
  • Associated symptoms: No dysuria, no haematuria, no suprapubic pain, no feeling of incomplete emptying
  • Obstetric history: Para 2 (2 normal vaginal deliveries, no complications)
  • Gynaecological history: Menopause age 52, not on HRT
  • Past medical history: Type 2 diabetes (well-controlled, HbA1c 48 mmol/mol), hypertension
  • Medications: Metformin 1g BD, amlodipine 5mg OD
  • Social history: Non-smoker, drinks 6 cups of tea/day, BMI 28

Examination

  • Abdominal examination: Soft, non-tender, no palpable bladder, no masses
  • Pelvic examination:
    • Inspection: Mild vaginal atrophy, no visible prolapse
    • Speculum: No significant prolapse, cervix atrophic (postmenopausal)
    • Bimanual: Small postmenopausal uterus, no adnexal masses
    • Cough stress test: Negative - no leakage on coughing
  • Neurological examination (lower limbs): Normal power, reflexes, sensation, gait

Investigations

  • Urine dipstick: Glucose ++, no blood, no protein, no leucocytes, no nitrites
  • Urine MC&S: No growth (sterile)
  • Bladder diary (3 days):
    • Daytime frequency: 12-15 times
    • Nocturia: 4 times/night
    • Urgency episodes: 8-10/day
    • Incontinence episodes: 3-4/day (all preceded by sudden urge)
    • Fluid intake: 2.5L/day (mostly tea)
  • Post-void residual volume: 40mL (normal <50mL)
  • HbA1c: 48 mmol/mol (well-controlled diabetes)

Questions

1. What is the diagnosis? Explain the pathophysiology and how you would differentiate this from stress urinary incontinence.

Diagnosis: Overactive Bladder Syndrome (OAB) with Urgency Urinary Incontinence (UUI)

Definition:

  • Overactive bladder syndrome (OAB): Urinary urgency (sudden compelling desire to pass urine that is difficult to defer) ± urge incontinence, usually with frequency (≥8 times/day) and nocturia (≥2 times/night), in the absence of UTI or other pathology
  • Urgency urinary incontinence (UUI): Involuntary urine leakage preceded by urgency

Classification:

  • OAB "dry": Urgency, frequency, nocturia WITHOUT incontinence (65% of OAB cases)
  • OAB "wet": Urgency, frequency, nocturia WITH urge incontinence (35% of OAB cases - this patient)

Pathophysiology:

OAB is caused by detrusor overactivity: Involuntary contractions of the detrusor muscle during bladder filling → sudden urge to void ± incontinence

Mechanisms:

  • Neurogenic (10-20%): CNS pathology disrupts normal bladder control
    • Examples: Stroke, Parkinson's disease, multiple sclerosis, spinal cord injury
  • Non-neurogenic/"idiopathic" (80-90%): No identifiable neurological cause
    • Possible mechanisms: Age-related changes, bladder outlet obstruction, bladder hypersensitivity, altered neurotransmitter signalling (acetylcholine)

Differentiation: Urge Incontinence vs Stress Incontinence

Feature Urge Incontinence (OAB) Stress Incontinence (SUI)
Trigger Sudden urge to void (no physical exertion needed) Physical exertion (coughing, sneezing, exercise)
Urgency YES - key feature NO urgency
Volume of leakage Variable - can be large volume Small-to-moderate volume
Frequency Increased (≥8 times/day) Normal frequency
Nocturia Common (≥2 times/night) Rare
Cough stress test Negative Positive (immediate leakage on coughing)
Pathophysiology Detrusor overactivity Pelvic floor weakness ± intrinsic sphincter deficiency
Typical patient Older, neurological disease, diabetes Vaginal deliveries, menopause, obesity, chronic cough

Why This is Urge Incontinence (Not Stress Incontinence):

  • ✓ Leakage preceded by sudden, overwhelming urge (key feature)
  • ✓ Leakage NOT related to physical exertion
  • ✓ Negative cough stress test
  • ✓ High frequency (12-15 times/day - normal is 4-7 times/day)
  • ✓ Severe nocturia (4 times/night - normal is 0-1)
  • ✓ No risk factors for SUI (only 2 normal vaginal deliveries, no forceps/tears)

Risk Factors Present in This Patient:

  • Age (68 years): OAB prevalence increases with age (affects 30-40% of women >65 years)
  • Type 2 diabetes: Diabetic neuropathy can affect bladder innervation
  • High caffeine intake (6 cups tea/day): Caffeine is a bladder irritant and diuretic
  • Postmenopausal: Oestrogen deficiency → urogenital atrophy → bladder hypersensitivity

2. Outline a stepwise management plan for this patient, including lifestyle modifications, conservative treatments, and pharmacological options.

Management of Overactive Bladder Syndrome (NICE 2019 Guidelines)

Step 1: Conservative Management (FIRST-LINE - Trial for 6 Weeks)

A. Lifestyle Modifications:

  • Reduce caffeine intake: Currently 6 cups tea/day → reduce to ≤2 cups/day
    • Caffeine is a bladder irritant and diuretic → worsens urgency/frequency
    • Switch to decaffeinated drinks
  • Reduce evening fluid intake: Avoid drinking 2-3 hours before bed to reduce nocturia
  • Optimize total fluid intake: Currently 2.5L/day - reduce to 1.5-2L/day (excessive fluids worsen frequency)
  • Avoid carbonated drinks and alcohol: Both are bladder irritants
  • Weight loss (if BMI >30): BMI 28 - encourage healthy weight maintenance
  • Treat constipation: Constipation worsens OAB symptoms

B. Bladder Training (FIRST-LINE TREATMENT):

  • Goal: Increase bladder capacity, reduce urgency episodes, regain voluntary control
  • Programme (supervised by continence nurse/physiotherapist):
    • Education: Explain normal bladder function, detrusor overactivity, rationale for bladder training
    • Scheduled voiding: Start with comfortable interval (e.g., every 2 hours), gradually increase by 15-30 minutes every 1-2 weeks
    • Urge suppression techniques: When urgency occurs, stop, sit/stand still, distract (count backwards, deep breathing), contract pelvic floor muscles, wait for urge to subside, then walk slowly to toilet
    • Avoid "just-in-case" voiding (voiding before feeling urge) - reinforces abnormal bladder behavior
    • Duration: Minimum 6 weeks (ideally 12 weeks)
  • Evidence: 60-80% improvement in urgency/frequency with bladder training

C. Pelvic Floor Muscle Training (Adjunct):

  • PFMT is first-line for SUI but also helps OAB (contracting pelvic floor inhibits detrusor)
  • Combine with bladder training for maximum benefit

D. Optimize Diabetes Control:

  • HbA1c 48 mmol/mol (good control) - continue metformin
  • Poorly controlled diabetes → polyuria → worsens frequency

Step 2: Pharmacological Treatment (If Conservative Fails After 6 Weeks)

A. Antimuscarinics (M3 Receptor Antagonists) - FIRST-LINE MEDICATION:

Mechanism: Block muscarinic (M3) receptors on detrusor muscle → reduce involuntary detrusor contractions

Drug Dose Notes
Oxybutynin immediate-release 2.5-5mg TDS Cheapest option but highest anticholinergic side effects
Oxybutynin modified-release (MR) 5-10mg OD Fewer side effects than IR formulation
Tolterodine 2mg BD or 4mg MR OD Lower side effect profile than oxybutynin IR
Solifenacin 5-10mg OD Once-daily dosing, well-tolerated

Antimuscarinic Side Effects (Anticholinergic Effects):

  • Common: Dry mouth (most common - 30%), constipation, blurred vision
  • Serious: Urinary retention (especially in elderly men), cognitive impairment (confusion, memory problems - particularly in elderly)
  • WARNING in elderly (this patient is 68): Antimuscarinics associated with increased dementia risk (MHRA warning) - use lowest effective dose, review regularly

Contraindications to Antimuscarinics:

  • Closed-angle glaucoma, myasthenia gravis, urinary retention, severe constipation, gastric retention
  • Caution in elderly: Risk of cognitive impairment, falls

B. β3-Adrenergic Agonist (Alternative to Antimuscarinics):

  • Mirabegron 25-50mg OD
  • Mechanism: Stimulates β3-adrenoceptors on detrusor muscle → bladder relaxation during filling
  • Advantages: NO anticholinergic side effects (no dry mouth, no cognitive impairment) - preferred in elderly patients
  • Side effects: Hypertension (monitor BP), tachycardia, headache, UTI
  • Contraindications: Severe uncontrolled hypertension (BP >180/110) - caution in this patient (has hypertension)
  • Cost: More expensive than antimuscarinics (may not be first-line due to cost)

C. Vaginal Oestrogen (Adjunct in Postmenopausal Women):

  • Does not directly treat OAB but improves urogenital atrophy → may reduce bladder hypersensitivity
  • Safe in this patient (no contraindications)

Step 3: Specialist Interventions (If Medications Fail)

Indications for Urogynaecology Referral:

  • Failure of conservative + 2 different antimuscarinic trials
  • Haematuria (exclude malignancy)
  • Recurrent UTIs
  • Suspected neurological cause
  • Pelvic organ prolapse

Specialist Treatment Options:

  • A. Botulinum Toxin A (Botox) Injections into Detrusor:
    • Mechanism: Blocks acetylcholine release → paralysis of detrusor muscle
    • Efficacy: 60-70% improvement, lasts 6-12 months (requires repeat injections)
    • Risk: Urinary retention (5-10%) - may require clean intermittent self-catheterization (CISC)
  • B. Percutaneous Tibial Nerve Stimulation (PTNS):
    • Weekly 30-minute sessions (12 weeks) - needle inserted near tibial nerve → neuromodulation of bladder control
    • Efficacy: 50-60% improvement, non-invasive
  • C. Sacral Neuromodulation (SNM):
    • Implantable device (pacemaker-like) that stimulates sacral nerves (S3) → modulates bladder control
    • Efficacy: 70-80% improvement
    • Invasive, expensive, reserved for refractory cases

Management Plan for This Patient:

  1. Immediate:
    • Refer to continence nurse/women's health physiotherapist for bladder training (6-week programme)
    • Lifestyle advice: Reduce caffeine to ≤2 cups/day, reduce evening fluids, aim 1.5-2L total/day
    • Prescribe vaginal oestrogen (e.g., Ovestin cream) for vaginal atrophy
  2. Review in 6 weeks: If inadequate response → start pharmacological treatment:
    • First choice (given age 68, cognitive risk): Mirabegron 25mg OD (no anticholinergic effects) - but check BP first (has hypertension)
    • Alternative: Oxybutynin MR 5mg OD or Solifenacin 5mg OD (lower anticholinergic burden than oxybutynin IR)
  3. Review in 4 weeks after starting medication: Assess efficacy + side effects, titrate dose if needed
  4. If 2 medication trials fail: Refer to urogynaecology for specialist interventions (Botox, PTNS, SNM)

Key Learning Points

  • Overactive bladder syndrome (OAB) = urinary urgency (sudden compelling desire to void) ± urge incontinence, usually with frequency (≥8/day) and nocturia (≥2/night), in the absence of UTI/pathology
  • Urgency urinary incontinence (UUI) = involuntary leakage preceded by urgency - caused by detrusor overactivity (involuntary detrusor contractions)
  • Differentiate from stress incontinence: UUI = leakage preceded by sudden urge (no exertion), SUI = leakage with physical exertion (no urgency)
  • Risk factors for OAB: Age, neurological disease (stroke, Parkinson's, MS), diabetes, high caffeine intake, menopause
  • First-line treatment: Bladder training (6 weeks) + lifestyle modifications (reduce caffeine, optimize fluids) - 60-80% improvement
  • Pharmacological options:
    • Antimuscarinics (oxybutynin, tolterodine, solifenacin): Block M3 receptors → reduce detrusor contractions
    • Side effects: Dry mouth (30%), constipation, cognitive impairment (especially elderly) - MHRA warning re: dementia risk
    • β3-agonist (mirabegron): NO anticholinergic effects - preferred in elderly, but monitor BP (can cause hypertension)
  • Vaginal oestrogen: Adjunct in postmenopausal women (improves urogenital atrophy)
  • Specialist interventions (if medications fail): Botox injections (60-70% success, risk urinary retention), percutaneous tibial nerve stimulation (PTNS), sacral neuromodulation (SNM)
  • Red flags requiring urgent referral: Haematuria (exclude malignancy), recurrent UTIs, suspected neurological cause, pelvic mass
