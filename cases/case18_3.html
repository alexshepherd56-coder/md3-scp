Case 18.3 – Acute Pelvic Pain (Benign Ovarian Disease)

Category: Medicine | Discipline: Obstetrics & Gynaecology | Setting: Emergency Department

Case

Miss Emma Patterson, a 24-year-old university student, presents to the Emergency Department at 2am with a 4-hour history of sudden-onset severe right iliac fossa (RIF) pain. The pain started while she was lying in bed watching TV and reached maximum intensity within 10 minutes. She describes it as sharp, constant, and rated 9/10 in severity. She has vomited three times since the pain started.

The pain does not radiate and is not related to movement, eating, or bowel function. She denies urinary symptoms, vaginal bleeding, or discharge. Her last menstrual period was 2 weeks ago (mid-cycle) and was normal. She is sexually active with one regular partner and uses the combined oral contraceptive pill. She denies any recent trauma or vigorous exercise.

She has no significant past medical history and takes only the COCP. She is a non-smoker and drinks alcohol socially. There is no family history of gynaecological conditions.

On Examination:
  • General: Appears distressed and in pain, lying still
  • Vital signs: BP 108/68 mmHg, HR 102 bpm, Temperature 37.2°C, RR 18/min, SpO₂ 99% on air
  • Abdominal examination:
    • Severe tenderness in right iliac fossa with guarding
    • No rebound tenderness or rigidity
    • Bowel sounds present but reduced
    • No palpable masses
    • Negative Rovsing's sign, negative psoas sign
  • Per vaginal examination: Severe right adnexal tenderness, cervical excitation negative, uterus normal size, no masses palpable on left

Investigations

Bedside:
  • Urine βhCG: Negative
  • Urine dipstick: No blood, no leucocytes, no nitrites
Bloods:
  • WCC: 11.2 × 10⁹/L (mildly elevated)
  • CRP: 8 mg/L (normal)
  • Hb: 135 g/L (normal)
Pelvic Ultrasound (transvaginal):
  • Right ovary: Enlarged (8cm diameter), containing a 7cm simple cyst
  • Reduced blood flow to right ovary on Doppler (compared to left)
  • "Whirlpool sign" visible - suggestive of torsion
  • Left ovary: Normal appearance with good blood flow
  • No free fluid in pouch of Douglas
  • Uterus: Normal

Questions

Question 1: What is the differential diagnosis for acute pelvic pain in a woman of reproductive age, and what features distinguish ovarian torsion from other causes?

Differential Diagnosis for Acute Pelvic Pain:

Gynaecological causes:

  • Ovarian cyst accident:
    • Torsion: Sudden severe pain, nausea/vomiting, reduced/absent Doppler flow, whirlpool sign
    • Rupture: Sudden pain that may improve, free fluid on USS, can cause haemorrhage if haemorrhagic cyst
    • Haemorrhage into cyst: Gradual onset pain, enlarging cyst on USS
  • Ectopic pregnancy: Positive βhCG, missed period, shoulder-tip pain, adnexal mass, free fluid
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID): Bilateral pain, fever, discharge, cervical excitation, raised WCC/CRP
  • Mittelschmerz: Mid-cycle, unilateral, mild-moderate pain, self-limiting
  • Endometriosis: Chronic cyclical pain, dysmenorrhoea, dyspareunia (less likely to present acutely)

Non-gynaecological causes:

  • Appendicitis: RIF pain, anorexia, fever, McBurney's point tenderness, positive Rovsing's sign
  • UTI/pyelonephritis: Dysuria, frequency, loin pain, positive urine dip
  • Renal colic: Colicky loin-to-groin pain, haematuria, restless patient
  • Inflammatory bowel disease: Diarrhoea, PR bleeding, weight loss, chronic history
  • Bowel obstruction: Vomiting, distension, absolute constipation, tinkling bowel sounds

Features Distinguishing Ovarian Torsion:

  • Onset: Sudden, severe pain reaching maximum intensity within minutes
  • Character: Constant, sharp, unilateral (usually right > left)
  • Associated features: Nausea and vomiting (\>70%), patient lies still (movement worsens pain)
  • Timing: Often mid-cycle or during ovulation (when corpus luteum cysts form)
  • Risk factors: Known ovarian cyst (\>5cm), previous torsion, ovarian hyperstimulation (IVF), pregnancy (first trimester)
  • Examination: Severe adnexal tenderness, tender adnexal mass may be palpable, cervical excitation usually negative
  • Bloods: WCC may be mildly elevated, CRP usually normal (helps exclude appendicitis/PID)
  • USS findings:
    • Enlarged ovary (\>5cm)
    • Reduced or absent Doppler blood flow (though normal flow does NOT exclude torsion)
    • "Whirlpool sign" - twisted vascular pedicle
    • Peripherally displaced follicles
    • Free fluid may be present

This patient's features suggesting ovarian torsion:

  • Sudden-onset severe RIF pain
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Mid-cycle timing (ovulation - when corpus luteum cysts form)
  • Negative pregnancy test (excludes ectopic)
  • Normal urine dip (excludes UTI)
  • USS: 7cm ovarian cyst with reduced Doppler flow and whirlpool sign
Question 2: What is the pathophysiology of ovarian torsion, and what is the management?

Pathophysiology of Ovarian Torsion:

  • Definition: Twisting of the ovary (and often fallopian tube) on its vascular pedicle, causing venous and lymphatic obstruction → oedema → arterial compromise → ischaemia → necrosis
  • Mechanism:
    • Normal ovary is mobile and suspended by infundibulopelvic ligament (ovarian vessels) and utero-ovarian ligament
    • Ovarian mass (cyst >5cm) increases weight and mobility → predisposes to twisting
    • Right ovary more commonly affected (longer pedicle, more mobile)
  • Ischaemic timeline:
    • Initially venous/lymphatic obstruction → oedema and congestion
    • Later arterial compromise → ischaemia
    • After 24-48 hours → irreversible necrosis
    • TIME-CRITICAL EMERGENCY - requires urgent surgery

Management of Ovarian Torsion:

Immediate Management:

  • Analgesia: IV opioids (morphine/fentanyl) + antiemetics
  • IV access: Two large-bore cannulas (in case of haemorrhage)
  • NBM: Prepare for theatre
  • Bloods: FBC, G&S, clotting
  • Urgent USS: Confirm diagnosis (though clinical suspicion sufficient for theatre if high)
  • Urgent senior gynaecology review

Definitive Management - Urgent Laparoscopy (within hours):

  • First choice: Laparoscopic detorsion (untwisting) + cystectomy
    • Untwist the ovary (regardless of appearance - even if black/necrotic)
    • Ovary often recovers even if appears necrotic
    • Conservative approach - preserves fertility
    • Remove/drain cyst if possible (cystectomy)
    • Consider oophoropexy (fixation) to prevent recurrence
  • Oophorectomy (removal of ovary): Only if:
    • Ovary clearly necrotic and non-viable after detorsion
    • Suspicion of malignancy (rare in young women)
    • Postmenopausal patient

Key Surgical Principles:

  • DO NOT delay surgery awaiting USS if clinical suspicion high
  • Ovarian conservation is the priority in women of reproductive age
  • Detorsion should be attempted even if ovary looks necrotic (can recover)
  • No risk of thromboembolism from detorsion - this is a myth

Complications of Delayed Treatment:

  • Ovarian necrosis → loss of ovary
  • Reduced fertility (loss of one ovary reduces ovarian reserve)
  • Peritonitis if necrotic ovary perforates
  • Sepsis

Prognosis:

  • If treated within 24 hours: Excellent chance of ovarian salvage
  • Ovarian function usually preserved if detorsion successful
  • Recurrence risk 10-15% (higher if underlying cyst not removed)

This patient's management plan:

  1. IV morphine + cyclizine for pain/vomiting
  2. IV access, bloods (FBC, G&S), NBM
  3. Urgent gynaecology referral
  4. Emergency laparoscopy for detorsion + right ovarian cystectomy
  5. Aim to preserve ovary and fertility
Question 3: How would the presentation differ if this were a ruptured ovarian cyst rather than torsion?

Ruptured Ovarian Cyst - Clinical Features:

Similarities to torsion:

  • Sudden-onset unilateral pelvic pain
  • Often mid-cycle (rupture of corpus luteum/follicular cyst)
  • Adnexal tenderness on examination

Differences from torsion:

  • Pain pattern:
    • Initial sharp pain at rupture
    • Pain often improves after rupture (vs torsion which worsens)
    • May become dull/achy as fluid irritates peritoneum
  • Severity: Usually less severe than torsion (mild-moderate vs severe)
  • Vomiting: Less common and less severe
  • Examination:
    • Mild-moderate adnexal tenderness (vs severe in torsion)
    • Generalised lower abdominal tenderness if free fluid present
    • Peritonism if significant haemorrhage
  • USS findings:
    • Free fluid in pouch of Douglas (blood/cyst fluid)
    • Collapsed/absent cyst (previously seen on scan) OR residual cyst
    • Normal Doppler flow to ovary (key difference from torsion)
    • No whirlpool sign

Types of Ruptured Cysts:

  • Simple/follicular cyst rupture: Minimal symptoms, small amount of free fluid, conservative management
  • Corpus luteum cyst rupture: Moderate pain, more free fluid, usually self-limiting
  • Haemorrhagic cyst rupture: Can cause significant intraperitoneal bleeding
    • Severe pain, peritonism, shoulder-tip pain
    • Haemodynamic instability if large bleed
    • May require laparoscopy/laparotomy

Management of Ruptured Ovarian Cyst:

  • Conservative (most cases):
    • Analgesia (NSAIDs/paracetamol, avoid opioids if possible)
    • Observation (4-6 hours)
    • Repeat FBC to monitor Hb if haemorrhagic
    • Discharge if stable with safety-netting
  • Surgical (laparoscopy) if:
    • Haemodynamic instability (significant bleeding)
    • Falling Hb requiring transfusion
    • Peritonism/severe pain not improving
    • Uncertain diagnosis (? ectopic pregnancy)

Summary - Torsion vs Rupture:

Feature Ovarian Torsion Ruptured Cyst
Pain severity Severe (9/10) Mild-moderate (4-7/10)
Pain pattern Constant, worsening Improves after initial rupture
Vomiting Prominent (\>70%) Less common
Free fluid on USS Absent or minimal Present (blood/fluid)
Doppler flow Reduced/absent Normal
Whirlpool sign Present Absent
Management Urgent surgery (detorsion) Usually conservative

Key Learning Points

  • Ovarian torsion = twisting of ovary on vascular pedicle → venous obstruction → oedema → arterial compromise → ischaemia → necrosis
  • TIME-CRITICAL EMERGENCY - requires urgent laparoscopy within hours to preserve ovary
  • Classic presentation: Sudden-onset severe unilateral pelvic pain + nausea/vomiting in young woman
  • Risk factors: Ovarian cyst >5cm, mid-cycle (corpus luteum), pregnancy, ovarian hyperstimulation, previous torsion
  • USS findings: Enlarged ovary, reduced Doppler flow (NOT always present), whirlpool sign, no free fluid
  • Management: Urgent laparoscopic detorsion + cystectomy - aim to preserve ovary even if appears necrotic
  • DO NOT delay surgery for USS if high clinical suspicion - ovarian salvage depends on time
  • Detorsion is safe - no risk of thromboembolism (old myth)
  • Ruptured cyst: Pain improves after rupture, free fluid on USS, normal Doppler, usually managed conservatively
  • Differential: Ectopic pregnancy (βhCG positive), appendicitis (anorexia, fever, Rovsing's sign), PID (bilateral, fever, discharge)
