Case 30.3 – Aplastic Anaemia [SDL]

Category: Medicine | Discipline: Haematology | Week: 30

Self-Directed Learning Overview

This self-directed learning case focuses on Aplastic Anaemia, a rare but serious condition characterized by pancytopenia (reduction in all blood cell lines) due to bone marrow failure. Understanding this condition is crucial as it represents a haematological emergency requiring prompt recognition and specialist management.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this SDL session, you should be able to:

  • Define aplastic anaemia and explain its pathophysiology
  • Identify the causes of aplastic anaemia (acquired vs inherited)
  • Recognize the clinical presentation and examination findings
  • Interpret the key investigation findings including blood film and bone marrow biopsy
  • Understand the severity classification and its prognostic significance
  • Outline the management options including supportive care, immunosuppression, and stem cell transplantation
  • Recognize the complications and understand the importance of early specialist referral

Case Scenario

A 32-year-old woman, Ms. Sarah Thompson, presents to the Emergency Department with a 3-week history of progressive fatigue, recurrent nosebleeds, and easy bruising. She has also noticed increased frequency of infections over the past month. She denies any significant past medical history but mentions she recently completed a course of chloramphenicol for a presumed bacterial infection 6 weeks ago.

On Examination:
  • General: Pale, multiple bruises on limbs
  • ENT: Petechiae on soft palate, dried blood in nares
  • Cardiovascular: Tachycardic (HR 110 bpm), flow murmur
  • Respiratory: Clear chest
  • Abdomen: No hepatosplenomegaly, no lymphadenopathy
  • Skin: Petechiae and ecchymoses, no rash

Initial Investigations

Full Blood Count:
  • Hb: 62 g/L (normal 115-165 g/L)
  • WCC: 1.8 × 10⁹/L (normal 4-11 × 10⁹/L)
  • Neutrophils: 0.4 × 10⁹/L (normal 2-7.5 × 10⁹/L) - Severe neutropenia
  • Platelets: 12 × 10⁹/L (normal 150-400 × 10⁹/L) - Severe thrombocytopenia
  • Reticulocytes: 8 × 10⁹/L (normal 50-100 × 10⁹/L) - Low (inappropriately low response to anaemia)
Blood Film: Pancytopenia with no abnormal cells. Red cells normocytic normochromic. No blasts seen.
Bone Marrow Biopsy: Hypocellular marrow with <10% cellularity. Reduced megakaryocytes, erythroid and myeloid precursors. Increased fat spaces. No malignant infiltration or fibrosis.

Key Topics to Explore

1. Definition and Pathophysiology

Definition:

Aplastic anaemia is a condition characterized by pancytopenia (anaemia, leukopenia, and thrombocytopenia) resulting from bone marrow failure with reduced or absent haematopoietic precursors, leading to hypocellular bone marrow.

Pathophysiology:

The underlying mechanism involves:

  • Destruction of haematopoietic stem cells: Usually T-cell mediated autoimmune destruction of pluripotent stem cells
  • Impaired stem cell function: Reduced proliferation and differentiation capacity
  • Abnormal bone marrow microenvironment: Defective stromal support for haematopoiesis

This results in:

  • Reduction in all three blood cell lines (red cells, white cells, platelets)
  • Hypocellular bone marrow replaced by fatty tissue
  • Normal remaining cells (unlike dysplastic changes in myelodysplasia)

2. Aetiology and Causes

Acquired Aplastic Anaemia (majority of cases):

Category Examples
Idiopathic 70% of cases - no identifiable cause
Drugs Predictable dose-related: Chemotherapy, radiotherapy
Idiosyncratic: Chloramphenicol, NSAIDs, gold, penicillamine, sulfonamides, carbimazole, phenytoin
Chemicals/Toxins Benzene, insecticides, organic solvents
Viral Infections Epstein-Barr virus (EBV), cytomegalovirus (CMV), HIV, hepatitis viruses (especially non-A, non-B, non-C hepatitis), parvovirus B19
Radiation Ionizing radiation exposure
Immune Disorders Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), eosinophilic fasciitis
Pregnancy Rare, may remit after delivery

Inherited Aplastic Anaemia (rare):

  • Fanconi anaemia: Autosomal recessive, associated with short stature, skeletal abnormalities, café-au-lait spots, renal abnormalities, increased cancer risk. Usually presents in childhood but can present in young adults
  • Dyskeratosis congenita: X-linked or autosomal, characterized by abnormal skin pigmentation, nail dystrophy, oral leukoplakia, pulmonary fibrosis
  • Shwachman-Diamond syndrome: Autosomal recessive, pancreatic insufficiency, skeletal abnormalities

3. Clinical Presentation

Clinical features depend on which cell line is most severely affected:

Cell Line Deficiency Clinical Features
Anaemia
(Low RBCs)
  • Fatigue, lethargy, weakness
  • Dyspnoea on exertion
  • Pallor, conjunctival pallor
  • Tachycardia, flow murmur
  • Dizziness, headache
Leukopenia
(Low WBCs)
  • Recurrent or severe infections
  • Bacterial infections (especially neutropenia)
  • Fungal infections (oral candidiasis)
  • Mouth ulcers, gingivitis
  • Pneumonia, sepsis
Thrombocytopenia
(Low Platelets)
  • Easy bruising, petechiae, purpura
  • Mucosal bleeding (epistaxis, gum bleeding)
  • Menorrhagia
  • GI bleeding, haematuria (severe cases)
  • Intracranial haemorrhage (rare but life-threatening)

Important Examination Findings:

  • NO hepatosplenomegaly or lymphadenopathy (absence is KEY - presence suggests alternative diagnosis like leukaemia or lymphoma)
  • Pallor
  • Petechiae, purpura, ecchymoses
  • Signs of infection
  • Look for congenital abnormalities if inherited cause suspected

4. Investigations and Diagnosis

Essential Investigations:

1. Full Blood Count (FBC):

  • Pancytopenia affecting all three cell lines
  • Anaemia - usually normocytic normochromic
  • Leukopenia with neutropenia
  • Thrombocytopenia
  • Low reticulocyte count (indicates bone marrow not responding appropriately to anaemia)

2. Blood Film:

  • Confirms pancytopenia
  • Normal morphology of remaining cells
  • No abnormal/blast cells (helps exclude leukaemia)

3. Bone Marrow Biopsy (ESSENTIAL for diagnosis):

  • Hypocellular marrow (usually <25% cellularity, often <10%)
  • Increased fat spaces replacing haematopoietic tissue
  • Reduced/absent megakaryocytes, erythroid and myeloid precursors
  • No malignant infiltration, fibrosis, or dysplasia

4. Additional Tests to Identify Cause:

  • Viral screens: EBV, CMV, HIV, hepatitis (A, B, C), parvovirus B19
  • Autoimmune screen: ANA, dsDNA (if SLE suspected)
  • Vitamin B12 and folate (to exclude other causes of pancytopenia)
  • Flow cytometry to exclude PNH (paroxysmal nocturnal haemoglobinuria) - CD55 and CD59 deficiency
  • Chromosome breakage test if Fanconi anaemia suspected
  • HLA typing (if transplant candidate)

5. Imaging:

  • Chest X-ray if infection suspected
  • CT if invasive fungal infection suspected

Diagnostic Criteria for Aplastic Anaemia:

Requires presence of at least two of three peripheral blood criteria:

  • Haemoglobin <100 g/L
  • Platelet count <50 × 10⁹/L
  • Neutrophil count <1.5 × 10⁹/L

PLUS hypocellular bone marrow on biopsy

5. Severity Classification

Severity classification is based on peripheral blood counts and bone marrow cellularity. This has important prognostic and therapeutic implications.

Severity Bone Marrow Cellularity Peripheral Blood Criteria (≥2 of the following)
Non-Severe Hypocellular Does not meet criteria for severe AA
Severe AA (SAA) <25% cellularity
(or 25-50% with <30% haematopoietic cells)
  • Neutrophils <0.5 × 10⁹/L
  • Platelets <20 × 10⁹/L
  • Reticulocytes <20 × 10⁹/L
Very Severe AA (vSAA) As per SAA As per SAA PLUS
Neutrophils <0.2 × 10⁹/L

Prognostic Significance:

  • Severe and very severe AA: High mortality without treatment (50% 1-year mortality). Require urgent specialist referral and aggressive treatment
  • Non-severe AA: May have more indolent course but still requires treatment and monitoring
  • Neutrophil count is the most important prognostic factor - severe neutropenia (<0.5) associated with high infection risk and mortality

6. Management

IMMEDIATE ACTIONS - Haematological Emergency:

  • Urgent haematology referral (same day for severe/very severe AA)
  • Admit to hospital if severe neutropenia or thrombocytopenia
  • Isolate if neutropenic (single room, protective environment)
  • Avoid IM injections (bleeding risk)
  • AVOID unnecessary transfusions before transplant assessment (alloimmunization reduces transplant success)

Supportive Care:

  • Transfusion support:
    • Red cell transfusions for symptomatic anaemia (keep Hb >70-80 g/L)
    • Platelet transfusions for bleeding or very low counts (<10 × 10⁹/L)
    • Use irradiated, leukodepleted blood products (reduce CMV transmission and alloimmunization)
    • Use single-donor platelets if transplant candidate
  • Infection prevention and treatment:
    • Neutropenic precautions (hand hygiene, avoid crowded areas, avoid raw food)
    • Prophylactic antibiotics (fluoroquinolone) if prolonged neutropenia
    • Antifungal prophylaxis (fluconazole or posaconazole)
    • Antibiotic therapy for neutropenic sepsis (broad-spectrum, e.g., piperacillin-tazobactam)
    • G-CSF (granulocyte colony-stimulating factor) may be used short-term for severe infections but NOT routinely
  • Remove causative agent if identified (stop offending drug, avoid toxin exposure)

Definitive Treatment:

1. Haematopoietic Stem Cell Transplantation (HSCT):

  • First-line for young patients (<40 years) with severe/very severe AA and matched sibling donor
  • Curative treatment with 75-90% long-term survival
  • HLA typing of patient and siblings should be performed urgently
  • Risks: Graft failure (5-10%), graft-versus-host disease (GVHD), infections, late effects
  • Conditioning regimen: Cyclophosphamide + ATG ± radiotherapy
  • Matched unrelated donor (MUD) transplant considered if no sibling donor and age <40-50 years

2. Immunosuppressive Therapy (IST):

  • For patients not eligible for transplant: Older age (>40), no matched donor, patient preference
  • Standard regimen:
    • Anti-thymocyte globulin (ATG): Horse ATG preferred over rabbit ATG
    • Cyclosporine: For 6-12 months, often longer
  • Response rate: 60-70% achieve transfusion independence
  • Response usually seen by 3-6 months
  • Risks: Serum sickness, infections, relapse (30-40%), clonal evolution to MDS/AML (10-15% at 10 years)
  • May add eltrombopag (thrombopoietin receptor agonist) to improve response rates

3. Second-line Treatment:

  • Repeat course of IST
  • Alternative donor HSCT (MUD or haploidentical)
  • Eltrombopag monotherapy

Treatment Algorithm:

Severe/Very Severe AA
        ↓
Age <40 + Matched Sibling Donor?
        ↓
   YES → HSCT (first-line)
        ↓
   NO → IST (ATG + Cyclosporine ± Eltrombopag)
        ↓
   No response at 6 months or relapse
        ↓
   Consider: Repeat IST, MUD transplant, or Eltrombopag

Long-term Monitoring:

  • Regular FBC monitoring
  • Monitor for relapse
  • Monitor for clonal evolution (MDS, PNH, AML) - annual bone marrow if IST-treated
  • PNH screening (flow cytometry) as PNH can develop in AA patients

7. Differential Diagnosis

Other causes of pancytopenia to consider:

Condition Distinguishing Features
Acute Leukaemia Blast cells on film/marrow, HYPERcellular marrow with abnormal cells
Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS) Dysplastic changes, variable cellularity, cytogenetic abnormalities
Hairy Cell Leukaemia Splenomegaly, hairy cells on film, positive tartrate-resistant acid phosphatase (TRAP)
Hypersplenism Splenomegaly, pooling/destruction in spleen, normal/hypercellular marrow
Megaloblastic Anaemia (severe) Macrocytosis, hypersegmented neutrophils, megaloblastic marrow, low B12/folate
Bone Marrow Infiltration Lymphoma, myeloma, metastatic carcinoma - abnormal cells on marrow, organomegaly common
Paroxysmal Nocturnal Haemoglobinuria (PNH) Haemolytic anaemia, dark urine, thrombosis, flow cytometry (CD55/CD59 deficiency)
SLE Multisystem features, autoimmune serology positive, normal/cellular marrow

Key Points to Distinguish Aplastic Anaemia:

  • Hypocellular bone marrow (not hypercellular like leukaemia)
  • No organomegaly (no hepatosplenomegaly or lymphadenopathy)
  • No abnormal cells on blood film or marrow
  • Normocytic anaemia (not macrocytic like B12/folate deficiency)

8. Prognosis and Complications

Prognosis Without Treatment:

  • Severe/Very Severe AA: ~50% mortality at 1 year, primarily from infection and bleeding
  • Non-severe AA: Better prognosis but progressive disease occurs in some

Prognosis With Treatment:

  • HSCT (matched sibling): 75-90% long-term survival, best outcomes in young patients treated early
  • Immunosuppressive therapy: 60-70% achieve haematological response, 5-year survival ~75%

Complications:

Acute Complications:

  • Infection: Leading cause of death, particularly fungal and bacterial sepsis in severe neutropenia
  • Bleeding: Intracranial haemorrhage, GI bleeding in severe thrombocytopenia
  • Transfusion-related: Alloimmunization, iron overload (with chronic transfusions), transfusion reactions

Long-term Complications:

  • Relapse: 30-40% after IST, usually within 2-5 years
  • Clonal evolution:
    • Myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS): 10-15% at 10 years
    • Acute myeloid leukaemia (AML): 5-10% at 10 years
    • Paroxysmal nocturnal haemoglobinuria (PNH): 10-20% develop PNH clone
  • Transplant-related: Chronic GVHD, secondary malignancies, infertility, organ toxicity
  • Cyclosporine toxicity: Renal impairment, hypertension, gingival hyperplasia

Factors Associated with Better Prognosis:

  • Younger age
  • Shorter duration of disease before treatment
  • Higher baseline counts (especially neutrophils and platelets)
  • Response to initial IST

Key Learning Points - Summary

  • Aplastic anaemia is characterized by pancytopenia and hypocellular bone marrow due to failure of haematopoiesis
  • Most cases are idiopathic (70%), but important causes include drugs (chloramphenicol, NSAIDs), chemicals (benzene), viruses (hepatitis, EBV), and inherited syndromes (Fanconi anaemia)
  • Clinical features reflect the deficiency of all three cell lines: anaemia (fatigue, pallor), neutropenia (infections), thrombocytopenia (bleeding/bruising)
  • Absence of hepatosplenomegaly and lymphadenopathy is a KEY examination finding that helps distinguish AA from leukaemia/lymphoma
  • Diagnosis requires bone marrow biopsy showing hypocellular marrow with no abnormal cells
  • Severity is classified based on blood counts with neutrophil count being the most important prognostic factor
  • Treatment depends on age and donor availability:
    • Young patients (<40) with matched sibling donor → HSCT (first-line)
    • Older patients or no donor → Immunosuppressive therapy (ATG + Cyclosporine)
  • Urgent haematology referral is essential for severe/very severe AA - this is a haematological emergency
  • Supportive care includes transfusion support, infection prevention, and neutropenic precautions
  • Long-term complications include relapse (30-40%), clonal evolution to MDS/AML (10-15%), and development of PNH
  • Prognosis without treatment is poor (50% 1-year mortality for severe AA), but with appropriate treatment, 75-90% long-term survival is achievable
